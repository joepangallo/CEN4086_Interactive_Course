🔥 Warm-Up Polls (no wrong answers — vote, then we discuss)

Poll 1: Your web store got slow at 2:07 AM. When should the team have found out?

2:07 AM, from a machine — that's the whole premise of monitoring. Metrics watch every second; alerts wake a human only when a rule crosses a line. "Customers as monitoring" is the most expensive detection system ever invented: by the time they tweet, you've been losing orders for hours. And 2 AM slowness DOES matter — that's peak time somewhere, and batch jobs, backups, and attackers all love the night shift.

Poll 2: Industry FinOps surveys keep estimating how much of a typical cloud bill is wasted. Your guess?

Around 30% is the recurring survey answer (estimates commonly land between 25% and 35%). The waste hides in boring places: instances left running nights and weekends, oversized "just in case" servers, storage volumes orphaned by deleted VMs. That's why cost management is an operations discipline, not just a finance one — engineers create the waste, so engineers must see the bill. You'll hunt exactly these three species in today's bill explorer.

Poll 3: A pager alert fires 40 times a night and is a false alarm 39 of those times. What happens to the on-call engineer within a month?

Alert fatigue is a documented failure mode: humans habituate to noise, so the 40th page gets the same glance as spam — including the one night it's real. Famous outages have post-mortems containing the sentence "the alert fired, but was ignored." The fix is engineering, not discipline: fewer, better alerts tuned to real incidents. You'll feel this trade-off yourself in the threshold tuner in Segment 1.

1 Monitoring: Metrics, Logs, Alerts & Dashboards

You can't manage what you can't see

Today's session starts from a blunt truth: once your systems live in a provider's data center, monitoring data is your only eyes. Four instruments matter. Metrics are numbers sampled on a schedule — CPU %, memory %, request rate, error rate, latency — cheap to store, perfect for graphs and math. Logs are timestamped event records ("order 8812 failed: card declined") — expensive and detailed, perfect for answering why after metrics tell you that. Dashboards arrange metrics for human eyes so the on-call brain can grasp system health in five seconds. Alerts are rules that watch metrics for you and page a human when a line is crossed — they're what lets forty engineers sleep while software stands guard.

One metric deserves special respect: latency percentiles. The average response time is a liar — if 95 requests take 100 ms and 5 take 8 seconds, the average (~495 ms) describes literally no one's experience. Operations teams track p95 or p99: "95% of requests finished faster than this." When today's dashboard shows p95 latency, it's showing the experience of your unluckiest customers-who-still-matter. A p95 of 2 seconds means 1 in 20 clicks feels broken — and those are often your biggest-cart users, because heavy accounts generate heavy queries.

Common misconception: "more alerts = more safety." Alerting is a signal-to-noise problem: set thresholds too low and you page people for noise until they stop reading (alert fatigue — Poll 3); set them too high and real incidents slip past. Every alert should be actionable — if the correct response to a page is "shrug and go back to sleep," the alert is a defect. Tune it below.

2 Scaling, Capacity Planning & the Live Dashboard

Scale up vs. scale out — the operations rerun

You met this pair in Session 1 and priced it in Session 3; today's session asks the operational question: which lever do you pull at 2 AM? Scaling up (a bigger machine) is simple but interrupts service during the resize, hits a hardware ceiling, and leaves you with one bigger single point of failure — it's usually a planned daytime decision, best for stateful systems like databases that hate being split. Scaling out (more copies behind the load balancer) is the emergency lever: no downtime, near-instant, and it's what autoscaling automates by watching metrics and adding/removing instances by rule. Scaling out only works because Session 10 made your services stateless — any copy can serve any request.

Capacity planning is scaling's calm sibling: forecast next quarter's demand from growth trends, seasonality (Harbor Lane's holiday rush), and marketing's launch calendar, then decide how much headroom to keep — enough that a spike doesn't topple you, not so much that Poll 2's waste statistics come true. Automation closes the loop: autoscaling rules, scheduled scale-downs at night, and runbooks — written, step-by-step responses to known situations ("memory alert → check for leak → rolling restart → open ticket"). A runbook is how the new hire handles at 3 AM what the senior engineer figured out at 3 PM. Your capstone adds one tonight.

3 Cost Management, SLAs, Audits & Support

The bill is a metric too

Session 1 promised "measured service"; today's session delivers the invoice. A cloud bill is an itemized ledger — every instance-hour, gigabyte-month, and million requests, metered and priced. That granularity is a gift (you can see exactly where money goes) and a hazard (nobody is forced to look). Cost management is the discipline of looking: budgets with automatic alerts ("page finance at 80% of forecast"), tagging resources by team and project so spend is attributable, and a monthly waste hunt for the classic three: idle compute left running around the clock, orphaned storage surviving its deleted VM, and over-provisioned resources sized for a fantasy workload. Chargebacks bill each department for its actual usage (its gentler sibling, showback, just displays the number) — the moment teams see their own line item, oversized dev servers mysteriously shrink.

SLA management turns Session 9's availability math into a vendor relationship: know each service's promised uptime (99.9% ≈ 43.8 minutes of allowed downtime a month), remember that chained dependencies multiply (two serial 99.9% services ≈ 99.8% together), monitor the provider's status page against your own metrics, and actually claim the service credits when SLAs are missed — providers rarely volunteer them. Audits are scheduled self-checks: security (who still has admin?), configuration (does prod match the documented architecture?), and cost (the waste hunt above). Support and ticketing round out today's toolkit: know your support tier and response times BEFORE the outage, and route every incident through a ticket — the ticket trail becomes tomorrow's runbook and next month's audit evidence.

Common misconception: "the provider manages the cloud, so we don't have to." Session 8's shared-responsibility line applies to operations too: the provider keeps the platform alive; the sizing, the alerts, the bill, and the 2 AM decisions above the line are permanently yours.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · MLOps & AIOps — running models like production services

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, while learning to operate cloud systems (Objectives 7–8), you learn MLOps, AIOps, and AI FinOps — how teams run AI in production, and which signals exist only because a model is in the loop.

An AI feature in production is a service PLUS a model — which means everything on today's dashboard still applies, and none of it is enough. MLOps is today's management discipline extended to that second half: keep the classic golden signals (latency, error rate, saturation) and add the model-specific ones no classic tool knows to collect: token spend per day, cost per request, refusal/error rate (how often the model declines, times out, or returns junk), and — the subtle one — answer quality scored against a fixed evaluation set: the same bank of questions with known-good answers, re-run on a schedule and on every model-version change. That eval score is the only way to catch silent drift — a model version updates or your data shifts, and answers quietly get worse while every uptime graph stays green. When the score slides from 91% to 78%, THAT is the page.

AIOps points the telescope the other way: AI watching your metrics. Today's tuner taught you why fixed thresholds are hard — too low pages all night, too high sleeps through incidents; learned baselines adapt instead. Anomaly detection learns each metric's normal rhythm — Tuesday lunch spikes, quiet Sunday mornings, month-end batch jobs — and flags "this pattern is weird" before any static line fires: 3 AM traffic that's five times normal for 3 AM yet still below the daytime threshold, or latency that quietly doubled over three weeks without one alert. AIOps also clusters alert storms (200 alerts, one root cause) into a single incident and flags the disk that will fill next Tuesday. Two honest cautions: a badly tuned anomaly model just automates alert fatigue — the tuner trade-off moves up a level, it never disappears — and an AI-drafted incident summary is a draft, not a record, which is why Lab B makes you audit one against the raw log.

FinOps for AI applies today's cost discipline to the newest meter: set token budgets with alerts like any other budget line; allocate cost per feature (tag which product feature burned which tokens — chargeback, reborn); cache frequent answers so "what are your store hours?" doesn't hit a metered model 4,000 times a day; and right-size the model — a small, cheap model for classifying support tickets, the frontier model only where quality measurably demands it. Right-sizing is cloud management's oldest lesson (the 6%-utilized 4xlarge from the bill explorer!) applied to intelligence.

Common misconception: "latency and uptime look green, so the AI feature is healthy." Classic dashboards cannot see quality: a recommender can serve confident nonsense at a beautiful 200 ms and 100% uptime, and token cost can double under flat traffic. 📝 Session Quiz — Story Problems

Ten workplace scenarios — the last two test today's AI concept. Pick the best answer; every question explains all four options after you answer. Counts toward the syllabus "Exercises/Quizzes" grade (30%).

Q1 · The dashboard that lied politely

Yuki runs operations for BentoBox Meals' ordering platform. Her dashboard's headline number, "average response time: 180 ms," has been green all week — yet support logged 61 complaints about a "frozen checkout." Digging in, she finds 94% of requests finish in ~120 ms while about 5% (heavy carts hitting a slow inventory query) take 6–9 seconds. Which monitoring change would have surfaced this problem immediately?

Answer: C. ✔ Percentiles describe the unlucky tail: with 5% of requests taking 6–9 s, p95 would read in the seconds — a screaming red panel — while the mean stays a soothing 180 ms because the fast majority drowns out the slow few. Averages hide exactly the customers who suffer most (big carts = heavy queries = your best buyers). ✘ A adds precision to the wrong statistic — a more precise lie. ✘ B is Poll 1's "customers as monitoring": slow, vague, and after the damage. ✘ D throws away signal; checkout wasn't erroring, it was slow — an error-rate-only view would stay green forever.

Q2 · Forty pages a night

Omar inherits on-call for Riverbend Software. The previous admin set a CPU alert at 60% "to be safe." It now pages roughly 40 times a night — almost always during harmless nightly backups — and Omar catches himself deleting pages without reading them. Last Tuesday, page #23 was a genuine database failure; it sat unread for three hours. What is the correct fix?

Answer: A. ✔ This is alert fatigue, and it's an engineering defect, not a character flaw: 39 false pages a night train any human to ignore page #23. The fix is fewer, truer alerts — page on what customers feel (latency, errors, availability), tune thresholds against history like today's tuner, and suppress the predictable backup spike. ✘ B blames the person for a system that made vigilance impossible; the next on-call will fail identically. ✘ C multiplies noise — the signal-to-noise ratio gets worse, not better. ✘ D is muting with extra steps: the real incident lands in the ignored channel too. An alert that's safe to ignore is a defect either way.

Q3 · Two systems, two levers

Priyanka's architecture at TicketNest has two hot spots before Saturday's on-sale: the stateless web/API tier (any server can answer any request) and the single PostgreSQL write master that all orders funnel through. She can prepare both tiers today. Which scaling plan matches each tier's nature?

Answer: D. ✔ Match the lever to the state: stateless web servers are interchangeable, so adding copies behind the load balancer is cheap, instant, and reversible — perfect for autoscaling into a spike. A single write master, though, can't simply be photocopied (two masters accepting the same seat purchase = double-sold tickets), so you grow it vertically, in a planned maintenance window before Saturday. ✘ A wastes the web tier's superpower and hits scale-up ceilings during the rush. ✘ B naively "scales out" the database write path, which requires re-architecture (sharding/replicas), not a Saturday-morning button press. ✘ C is exactly backwards on both counts.

Q4 · The 40→92% staircase

At Big Cypress Outfitters, fleet memory has climbed 40% → 92% over six hours — a straight staircase since yesterday's 2 PM deploy of release 4.7. Restarting a node drops its memory to 40%, then the climb resumes. CPU and traffic are normal. The on-call, Dre, must pick tonight's plan. Which is right?

Answer: B. ✔ The fingerprint (steady climb, reset by restart, started with a deploy, traffic normal) is a memory leak, and the discipline is mitigate vs. root cause: rolling restarts keep customers unaffected tonight, while rolling back 4.7 removes the defect that restarts merely postpone. ✘ A treats a software bug with a hardware invoice — doubled RAM doubles the time-to-crash and the bill, forever. ✘ C ships the leak to the standby region: the bug is in the release, and the release deploys there too. ✘ D ignores that leaks don't stabilize; at ~98% the out-of-memory killer starts executing processes, and Dre's quiet night becomes an outage call.

Q5 · The volumes nobody owned

Auditing Mangrove Media's bill, Fatima finds a $410/month line: "block storage volumes — 22 units, 5.1 TB." Cross-checking, she discovers 19 of the 22 volumes are attached to nothing — they belonged to VMs deleted during last year's re-platforming; the delete dialog's "also delete storage" box was never checked. The volumes hold old boot disks nobody has mounted in 14 months. What is this, and what's the fix?

Answer: C. ✔ Orphaned volumes are the classic waste species (one of your three finds in the bill explorer): storage outlives its VM by default in most clouds, silently billing forever. The professional fix keeps a safety net — snapshot to archive tier (pennies) before deleting — then prevents recurrence with policy/automation. ✘ A flatters the mistake: real backups are deliberate, tested, and lifecycle-managed; accidental leftovers are none of those. ✘ B — the provider metered exactly what was provisioned; the meter is honest, the housekeeping wasn't. ✘ D has no supporting evidence; the boring explanation (an unchecked checkbox, 22 times) is the true one.

Q6 · Making the meter visible

CloudWays' CFO, Selma, is furious: cloud spend doubled in a year, and when she asks engineering managers why, each shrugs — "that's the platform team's bill." The platform team, in turn, can't say which product lines drive the cost. Selma mandates resource tagging by team, monthly cost reports per department, and — starting next quarter — each department's cloud usage charged against its own budget. What is Selma implementing, and what's the realistic expected effect?

Answer: A. ✔ Reports-only is showback; charging budgets is chargeback — and the sequence Selma chose (visibility first, accountability second) is the recommended rollout. The effect is well documented: when the team that provisions the oversized instance also eats its cost, rightsizing stops being the platform team's nagging and becomes self-interest. ✘ B is a blunt instrument that punishes growth products and creates shadow IT; nothing in her mandate freezes anything. ✘ C involves provider switching costs — unrelated to internal cost attribution. ✘ D changes what the provider promises and charges; Selma's problem is internal: nobody owns the meter. Tags + budgets fix ownership, not vendor terms.

Q7 · The 99.99% brochure

Marketing at PayLagoon wants to advertise "99.99% uptime" for the new checkout API. Ops lead Viktor points out the checkout's critical path chains TWO dependencies in series: the identity service (SLA 99.9%) and the payments processor (SLA 99.9%) — if either is down, checkout is down. Ignoring everything else, what availability can the chain honestly support, and what should Viktor tell marketing?

Answer: B. ✔ Serial dependencies multiply: 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.998001 ≈ 99.8%, about 87 minutes of expected monthly downtime — nearly TWENTY times the ~4.4 minutes that 99.99% permits. Advertising 99.99% would commit PayLagoon to paying SLA credits for math it never controlled. ✘ A takes the maximum, but failures are independent events — either failing kills checkout, so risk adds, not filters. ✘ C is the subtle trap: 99.9% would hold only if both services failed at exactly the same moments; independent failures make the chain weaker than its weakest link. ✘ D — this math is precisely knowable; that's why Session 9 made you do it. Honest options: redundancy per dependency, or an honest number.

Q8 · Every Sunday at 3 AM

For 14 consecutive Sundays, DockPilot's on-call has been paged at ~3 AM: a log partition fills, the fix is the same documented five commands (compress, ship to object storage, clear, verify, close ticket), takes 20 minutes, and has never varied. The team lead, Ana, is reviewing the on-call load. What should she do with this alert-and-response pair?

Answer: D. ✔ Today's automation principle: a response that is documented, identical, and safe 14 times running is a script, not a human's night. Automate it (better: a log-rotation/lifecycle policy that prevents the fill entirely), and alert only on the exception — automation failed, or the pattern changed. That's how on-call capacity gets reserved for problems that need judgment. ✘ A romanticizes toil; fatigue makes engineers worse at the incidents that DO need them. ✘ B removes the safety net without removing the risk — the disk still fills, now silently. ✘ C polishes the wrong artifact: a perfect runbook for a task no human should perform is documentation of waste. Runbooks are for judgment calls and for automation's edge cases.

Q9 · Green dashboards, angry readers 🧠 AI concept

PalmReads' AI "next book" recommender launched behind full classic monitoring: uptime 100%, p95 latency 210 ms, 5xx error rate 0.02% — green for eleven straight weeks. In week twelve, support tickets triple: "the recommendations turned useless." Investigation reveals the model provider shipped a new model version in week nine; reply quality degraded immediately, but every dashboard stayed green because requests kept succeeding, quickly. Which monitoring was missing?

Answer: B. ✔ This is silent drift, the failure mode classic monitoring is structurally blind to: every request succeeded, fast — the answers were just worse. Only comparing outputs against a fixed eval set detects that, which is why MLOps treats eval scores as a first-class production metric and re-runs them whenever the model version changes. ✘ A measures speed more precisely; the replies were fast and bad — no percentile of a healthy latency distribution encodes answer quality. ✘ C monitors infrastructure PalmReads doesn't operate and can't see — and the provider's fleet was healthy; the model changed, nothing broke. ✘ D is the exact misconception this session kills: three weeks of tickets weren't coincidence, they were the only quality monitor PalmReads had — customers-as-monitoring, the most expensive detection system ever invented (Poll 1).

Q10 · The bill that doubled quietly 🧠 AI concept

Gullwing Travel's AI trip-planner served the same ~60,000 requests in April as in March — traffic flat, features unchanged, per-token prices unchanged. Yet April's token invoice is 2.1× March's. CFO Elena asks ops lead Mateo for the likely cause and the controls. What should Mateo answer?

Answer: C. ✔ Token cost = requests × tokens-per-request × price. With requests and price flat, the factor that grew is tokens per request — and prompt bloat is the usual culprit: someone enriched the system prompt, or each call now ships the full conversation history or a fatter retrieval context. The AI-FinOps controls mirror today's cost discipline: watch the meter (cost per request), right-size (trim, cap, and match model size to task), and cache the repeats. ✘ A skips diagnosis — the meter is almost always honest; check your own token telemetry before accusing the vendor's. ✘ B invents an escalator that doesn't exist: per-token prices have generally fallen, and contracts don't auto-double. ✘ D — far from impossible, this is the standard AI-FinOps incident; cost per request is exactly the metric that catches it, which is why it's on today's list.
