1 SaaS: Renting Finished Software

What Software as a Service actually is

SaaS delivers a complete, finished application over the internet, billed as a subscription (usually per user per month), with the provider running everything underneath — servers, storage, the application code, patching, upgrades, backups, uptime. You bring a browser, your data, and your users. Compare that to how software shipped for decades: you bought Office on discs, installed it on every PC, applied service packs, and bought the next version in three years. With Google Workspace or Microsoft 365, there is no install, no version number to chase, and no server in your building; you log in and the newest version is simply there, for every employee, on every device.

Why it matters: SaaS turned software from a capital purchase into an operating expense (the CapEx→OpEx shift from Session 1, now applied to applications), collapsed deployment time from months to minutes, and moved the burden of keeping software secure and current onto a vendor whose entire business depends on doing it well. It's why a 12-store bookstore can run the same email and CRM technology as a Fortune 500 — paying only for 240 seats instead of an IT department.

Common misconception: "SaaS just means a website." Not quite. A static marketing page isn't SaaS. SaaS is software functionality — email, CRM, accounting, design tools — consumed as a metered, account-based service. The tell: you'd lose real work capability if your subscription lapsed.

Multi-tenancy: the apartment building

Here is the economic engine of SaaS. Multi-tenancy means ONE running application and ONE pool of infrastructure serve MANY customers ("tenants") at once, with each tenant's data logically isolated. Think apartment building: every tenant shares the foundation, elevator, plumbing, and security guard — but each apartment has its own locked door. In software terms: customers share the codebase, servers, and database cluster, while a tenant_id on every row, enforced by access controls and encryption, keeps each customer's data private. The vendor pays for the building once and divides the cost across hundreds or thousands of tenants — which is why SaaS can cost $6–15 per user per month. Sharing has a second superpower: when the vendor upgrades the "elevator" (ships a new feature or security fix), every tenant on every floor gets it simultaneously. No versions in the wild, no customers stranded on 2019 builds.

The alternative, single-tenancy, gives one customer a dedicated instance — their own copy of the app and database, sometimes their own hardware. More isolation, more customization room, easier compliance conversations… and much higher cost, plus upgrades that arrive on your schedule instead of continuously. Vendors often sell it as a premium tier to banks and hospitals.

Misconception to kill now: "multi-tenant means my data is mixed up with my competitor's." Logically isolated is not the same as commingled — proper tenant isolation is enforced on every query, and a tenant can no more read a neighbor's rows than an apartment tenant can wander into the unit upstairs. The legitimate concerns are different ones: noisy neighbors (a tenant's load affecting shared performance) and the blast radius of a vendor-side bug — both of which the vendor engineers against.

OpenSaaS: SaaS with an exit door

OpenSaaS is SaaS built on open-source software. You still get the convenience of a hosted, vendor-run service — but because the underlying code is open and the data formats are standard, you keep options a proprietary SaaS never offers: export your data cleanly, self-host the very same software, or move to a different hosting company running the same stack. The classic example pattern: WordPress.com is a hosted service, but it runs open-source WordPress — if the relationship sours, you can take your content to any other WordPress host or your own server. Why it matters: it directly softens vendor lock-in, the risk that switching costs trap you (a theme that returns loudly in the PaaS segment). Misconception: OpenSaaS is not free — you're still paying for hosting, operations, and support. The "open" refers to the source code and your freedom to leave, not the price tag.

2 The SaaS Business Case — Run the Numbers

Advantages (and for whom)

For the customer: near-zero upfront cost; deployment measured in minutes ("create 240 accounts" vs. "install on 240 PCs"); automatic, continuous upgrades; access from any device anywhere; predictable per-seat billing that scales down as well as up — when four employees leave, you stop paying for four seats. For the vendor (their side of the deal matters too): recurring subscription revenue instead of boom-bust license sales, a single codebase to maintain instead of six supported versions, and usage telemetry that shows exactly which features matter. Understanding the vendor's advantages explains vendor behavior — like why they'll happily give you month one free (they're buying an annuity), and why true per-customer customization is against their economic religion.

Disadvantages — every one is a design constraint, not a dealbreaker

(1) Internet dependency: no connection, no software — mitigated by offline modes and redundant links, but never zero. (2) Limited deep customization: one shared codebase means you get configuration options, not custom core code; if your 14-step bespoke workflow must survive exactly as-is, SaaS will frustrate you. (3) Data control: your data lives in the vendor's systems, under their security and their jurisdiction — contracts, encryption, and export rights are your levers. (4) The forever subscription: a license you bought once is yours; a subscription never ends. Over enough years, at enough seats, renting can cost more than owning — which is exactly why professionals model it instead of guessing.

3 PaaS: Renting the Platform

What "platform" means

Between raw rented hardware (IaaS, next session) and finished rented software (SaaS, this morning) sits a third deal: Platform as a Service. The provider runs everything your code needs to execute — operating system, language runtime, web server, middleware, databases, networking — and you supply only the application code and its data. "Platform" is precisely that bundle: the stage your app performs on, pre-built, patched, and scaled by someone else. A useful test: with PaaS, there is no server you can log into, because the server is not your problem. Deshawn's team (you'll meet them in the quiz) doesn't know or care which machine runs their ordering app — they know git push makes the new version live.

The three jobs a PaaS does: build → deploy → manage

Today's segment breaks the platform's work into a lifecycle. Build: the platform detects your language, installs dependencies, compiles/packages the app, and runs your tests — the same steps every time, so "works on my machine" dies. Deploy: it rolls the new version out with zero downtime (start the new copy, health-check it, swap traffic, keep the old one for instant rollback) and gives it a URL. Manage/scale: it watches load and adds or removes instances automatically, aggregates logs, restarts crashes. Why it matters: those three jobs used to be a sysadmin's whole week. PaaS compresses them into a minute of automation, which is why small teams whose differentiator is code — not infrastructure — love it. Misconception: "PaaS means no ops at all." You still own your app's performance, bugs, data model, and the bill; you've outsourced the stage crew, not the show.

The fine print: vendor lock-in

Let's be honest about PaaS's biggest disadvantage. Platforms win your love with proprietary conveniences — their special queue service, their custom database API, their auth SDK. Each one you adopt saves a week now and welds your app to that vendor, because leaving later means rewriting every welded part. That's vendor lock-in: switching costs so high that the vendor gains pricing power over you — remember it when a renewal quote arrives 40% higher. Mitigations are chosen at build time, not exit time: prefer open standards (plain SQL, standard protocols, containers), isolate vendor-specific code behind your own interfaces, and price the exit into the purchase decision. Note the rhyme with OpenSaaS from Segment 1 — in every model, the professional question is the same: "how would we leave?" Other PaaS trade-offs: less control over runtime versions (the platform upgrades when it upgrades) and constraints on what unusual software you can run at all.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · LLMs & Prompting — the SaaS you talk to, the PaaS you build on

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, while mastering Objectives 2–3, you learn what a large language model actually is — and why the same model reaches you two ways: as a chatbot (SaaS) and as a model API (PaaS).

When ChatGPT reached an estimated 100 million users about two months after launch — the fastest consumer-app adoption ever recorded at the time — what actually launched was a picture-perfect SaaS product: it runs in a browser, bills as a monthly subscription, ships silent continuous updates, and the provider operates every layer down to the GPUs. It is also multi-tenancy at a scale the early SaaS pioneers never imagined: millions of concurrent users share the same model-serving fleets, isolated logically per account, with the staggering hardware cost divided across all of them. Gemini, Claude, and Copilot follow the identical pattern. When you evaluated "email and CRM" as SaaS this morning, you were using the same checklist that applies to an AI assistant subscription: per-seat price, data handling, export rights, uptime, customization limits.

Now the PaaS twist: those same models are also sold as platform services. A developer gets an API key and calls the model from her code, paying per token — she brings her application, the provider runs the "model platform" (hosting, scaling, updating the model). That's Segment 3's deal with new paint. And Segment 3's warning tags along: build your whole product on one vendor's proprietary model APIs and prompt behaviors, and switching later costs real engineering — AI lock-in is PaaS lock-in reborn. Teams mitigate it the same way: standard interfaces, abstraction layers, an exit plan priced in from day one.

The concept: a next-token predictor with a finite working memory

Strip away the chat bubble and a large language model (LLM) is one function applied over and over: given all the text so far, predict the next token. A token is a chunk of text, roughly ¾ of an English word. The model's billions of parameters (learned during training — Session 1's story) encode the patterns of language; at inference your prompt flows through them, a probability emerges for every possible next token, one is picked, and the loop repeats — the reply assembles token by token. Nothing is looked up; everything is generated.

The text a model can consider at once is its context window — its working memory, measured in tokens and strictly finite (a few thousand to a few hundred thousand, depending on the model). Each turn, the model re-reads the conversation — but only the part that still fits. In a marathon chat the earliest messages eventually slide out, which is why a support bot can "forget" the order number you gave it an hour ago. Nothing was deleted — there is no memory bank; the text simply no longer fits through the model's reading slot. Chat products bolt memory on (summaries of old turns, saved profile notes, quietly re-inserted) — vendor engineering, not the model remembering.

Why does the same question get different wording every time? Because the model outputs probabilities, and the serving system samples from them. Temperature is the dial: low temperature ≈ always take the front-runner token — consistent and predictable, right for a billing bot; high temperature lets underdog tokens win sometimes — varied and creative, right for brainstorming. The chatbot vendor positions that dial for you (SaaS gives you settings); the model API exposes it to developers (PaaS gives you knobs).

And before your first word arrives, the vendor has already spoken. A system prompt — invisible instructions prepended to every conversation ("You are OrbitDesk's assistant. Discuss only help-desk topics. Never promise refunds.") — sets the bot's persona and guardrails. It is the SaaS configuration layer, Segment 1 in miniature: one shared multi-tenant model underneath, per-product behavior set by configuration rather than custom core code. Build on the API instead, and writing the system prompt becomes your job — the platform hands that layer to you.

Misconception to bust: "the chatbot understands and remembers me like a person." It predicts tokens inside a finite window. It doesn't know you between chats unless product code re-feeds saved notes, and it doesn't "recall" the conversation — it re-reads whatever still fits. Treat the warmth as interface design, not friendship — and never as a reason to skip verifying its claims.

Q1 · Nine hundred florists, one database

Tessa starts as a junior engineer at BloomBase, a SaaS point-of-sale product used by 900 flower shops. On day one she discovers all 900 customers run on ONE shared application deployment and one database cluster, with a tenant_id column separating each shop's rows. Alarmed, she messages her manager: "Shouldn't every customer get their own copy of the app?" Her manager grins: "That would end the company." What is this architecture called, and why does BloomBase bet the business on it?

Answer: B. ✔ One running application plus pooled infrastructure serving many logically isolated customers is multi-tenancy — the economic engine of SaaS. Infrastructure, patching, and development are paid for once and divided 900 ways, and version N+1 ships to every florist simultaneously; isolation is enforced logically (tenant IDs, access controls, encryption), not with separate hardware. ✘ A is the opposite architecture: single-tenancy gives each customer a dedicated instance — more control and more cost, typically sold as a premium tier; 900 dedicated instances would indeed "end the company." ✘ C — grid computing chops one huge computation into parallel pieces; BloomBase serves many small independent workloads, the reverse shape. ✘ D — a private cloud dedicates infrastructure to ONE organization; 900 shops sharing a vendor's platform is precisely not that, and the florists own nothing.

Q2 · The workflow that wouldn't bend

Grimaldi & Voss, a 60-lawyer firm, adopts a popular SaaS practice-management suite at $59/user/month. Six weeks in, the partners demand the intake screens be rebuilt to mirror the firm's bespoke 14-step conflict-check process, exactly as their old custom system did it. The vendor replies: configuration options exist — custom fields, templates, toggles — but changes to core workflow code ship to ALL customers or none, and a private fork is impossible. Which SaaS characteristic did the firm collide with?

Answer: D. ✔ The same shared-codebase economics that make SaaS affordable also constrain it: the vendor exposes configuration, but the core product must stay one version for all tenants, so process-level custom code is usually off the table. Firms with truly bespoke workflows either adapt the process, pay for an extensibility/premium tier, or build their own app (often on PaaS — Segment 3's territory). ✘ A — connectivity isn't the complaint here; the suite is up and running fine. ✘ B — the pricing model has nothing to do with workflow rigidity; the partners are angry about screens, not invoices. ✘ C — MFA is a security control, unrelated to customization. This cost-and-currency versus control trade-off is THE recurring SaaS decision — it's waiting for you in the capstone SaaS-stack recommendation.

Q3 · Run the numbers, Ren

Ren must present the 3-year cost of email + docs for Solstice Outfitters' 150 staff. Option 1, SaaS suite: $11/user/month. Option 2, on-prem: $310/user perpetual licenses, $14,000 in server hardware, plus a support contract averaging 20% of total license cost per year. Using 36 months and the method from today's calculator, which option is cheaper, and by roughly how much?

Answer: A. ✔ Do it in three lines. SaaS: 150 × $11 × 36 = $59,400. On-prem: licenses 150 × $310 = $46,500; support 20%/year × 3 years = $27,900; hardware $14,000 → $88,400. Gap ≈ $29,000 in SaaS's favor. ✘ B reverses the result — and "owning always wins" is exactly the assumption the arithmetic exists to test. ✘ C only looks true if you forget the support contract and hardware — the two lines buyers most often omit; licenses alone ($46,500) versus SaaS ($59,400) is closer, but that comparison is fiction. ✘ D confuses the on-prem total with the difference — a classic slide-deck error that a CFO will catch instantly. Note the model still omits on-prem admin labor, which would widen the gap further.

Q4 · The exit door

After a bruising migration OUT of a proprietary CRM — the data export alone cost $18,000 in consulting fees and arrived as half-corrupted CSVs — CTO Amara writes one non-negotiable rule for the next SaaS purchase: the product must be built on open-source software with standard data formats, so her company could self-host the same software or switch hosts if the vendor relationship sours. What is Amara demanding?

Answer: C. ✔ OpenSaaS keeps SaaS's convenience (a vendor hosts and operates the software) while the open-source base and standard formats keep the exit door open: export cleanly, self-host the identical software, or move to another host running the same stack — the WordPress.com/WordPress pattern from today. It softens lock-in; it doesn't erase migration effort. ✘ A — community cloud is a deployment model (allied orgs sharing infrastructure, like Session 1's hospitals); it says nothing about source-code openness. ✘ B — IaaS rents raw machines; Amara still wants finished software, just with portability guarantees. ✘ D — she explicitly wants a hosted, supported product; OpenSaaS is still paid SaaS. "Open" describes the code underneath and her freedom to leave — not the price.

Q5 · Push and pray no more

Deshawn's four-developer team at Kettle & Crumb built the bakery chain's custom online-ordering app. Today, every release means a Saturday of manually copying files onto a rented virtual machine, editing configs by hand, and praying. They employ no sysadmin and never want to. They move to a service where git push automatically triggers dependency install, tests, a zero-downtime rollout, and traffic-based scaling. What did they adopt — and what do they still manage afterward?

Answer: B. ✔ "Push code and the platform builds, deploys, and scales it" is the definition of PaaS, and the dividing line is exactly Segment 3's: everything below the application (runtime, middleware, OS, virtualization, hardware) becomes the provider's job, while the app's code quality, bugs, data model, and monthly bill remain the team's. ✘ A fails twice: SaaS would mean renting someone else's finished ordering product rather than deploying their own code — and even SaaS customers still manage data, users, and settings, so "nothing at all" is never true. ✘ C describes the miserable Saturday they just escaped — the rented VM where OS care was their problem was IaaS. ✘ D — nothing moved into their building; hardware went further away, not closer. The stages they gained are the ones you clicked in today's pipeline simulator.

Q6 · The golden handcuffs

Two years after building its logistics app on the CloudNimbus PaaS, Vantage Freight receives a renewal quote 40% higher. Shopping around, the CTO discovers the app uses CloudNimbus's proprietary message-queue service, its non-standard database API, and its custom authentication SDK; engineers estimate seven months of rewriting to run anywhere else. The account rep, reviewing the same architecture, does not seem worried about losing the deal. What is this situation called, and what would have reduced it?

Answer: C. ✔ Lock-in = switching costs so high the vendor gains pricing power — Segment 3's headline PaaS disadvantage. Each proprietary convenience (special queue, custom DB API, auth SDK) saved a week at build time and welded the app to CloudNimbus; seven months of rewrite is the rep's negotiating leverage. Mitigations are chosen when building, not when leaving: standard protocols and SQL, containers/portable frameworks, vendor-specific code isolated behind your own interfaces, and an exit cost estimated before signing. ✘ A — tenancy describes how customers share infrastructure; single-tenant products can lock you in just as hard. ✘ B — elasticity is capacity automatically tracking demand; turning off autoscaling changes a bill, not bargaining power. ✘ D — OpenSaaS is nearly the opposite (openness that preserves exits), and lock-in absolutely can be reduced — by the decisions Vantage skipped.

Q7 · The server in the closet retires

Harbor Lane Books' Exchange email server (installed 2016) shares the famous closet with everything else. It demands monthly security patches, needs a $9,000 hardware refresh this year, and crashed during the last two author-event weekends. Ops manager Coral proposes SaaS email at $6/user/month for all 240 staff. The owner asks the right question: "Tell me exactly what stops being OUR problem — and what doesn't." What is the accurate answer?

Answer: A. ✔ In SaaS the provider operates the entire stack — infrastructure, application patching, spam/malware filtering, redundant storage, uptime backed by an SLA. What never outsources: identity and account administration (provisioning new hires, cutting off leavers — Session 4's whole topic), policy choices like retention and sharing rules, and accountability for the company's own data. ✘ B overshoots — if Harbor Lane doesn't decide who gets mailboxes, nobody does; governance can't be rented. ✘ C is flatly wrong: the monthly patch grind, the $9,000 refresh, and the event-weekend crashes genuinely leave the building — that's the entire value proposition. ✘ D describes IaaS ("your software on rented machines"), not SaaS; in SaaS, patching the application is precisely the provider's job. Coral's proposal returns in your capstone cost table.

Q8 · Three quotes on the whiteboard

Nadia's six-person startup is building a custom subscription-box web app — the code IS the business. They have zero infrastructure staff and $40,000 of runway. On the whiteboard: (1) buy two servers for the office closet; (2) rent IaaS VMs and self-manage OS, runtime, and deploys; (3) adopt an established SaaS storefront that allows no custom code; (4) deploy their own code on a PaaS with automated build, deploy, and scaling. Which option fits BEST, and why?

Answer: D. ✔ This is the PaaS sweet spot in one scenario: a team whose value is proprietary code, with no appetite or budget for infrastructure operations. Build/deploy/scale automation turns the missing sysadmin from a crisis into a non-issue, and runway pays developers instead of racks. The price of D is Q6's lock-in risk — manageable with the mitigations you just named. ✘ A is the worst fit: capital expense burns scarce runway, and closet servers add facilities risk, no elasticity, and a single point of failure. ✘ B erases their differentiator — SaaS fits commodity needs (email, docs, CRM), not the product itself; a no-custom-code storefront makes them a reseller, not a startup. ✘ C — IaaS control costs exactly the resource they lack: staff hours for OS patching, deploy scripts, and scaling configs; control only "wins" when you need it AND can staff it.

Q9 · The goldfish support agent 🧠 AI concept

Leena manages support at VellumWorks, which licenses an AI chat agent (SaaS). A customer spends 90 minutes troubleshooting a printer; in message 4 she gave her order number and printer model. After an hour of pasted log files, the bot cheerfully asks: "Could you tell me your printer model?" The customer is livid, and Leena must explain to her boss what technically happened. What's the accurate explanation?

Answer: C. ✔ An LLM has no memory bank — every reply is generated from whatever text currently fits in its finite context window, measured in tokens. Ninety minutes of chat plus bulky pasted logs is many thousands of tokens; the earliest turns slid out of the window, so to the model they no longer exist. Nothing was "deleted" — it was never stored as memory in the first place. (Chat products mitigate this by summarizing older turns or saving key facts outside the chat — vendor engineering, not model memory.) ✘ A invents a policy; the bot still recalled recent messages perfectly, which scheduled deletion wouldn't explain. ✘ B confuses Session 1's two phases — training happens rarely, offline, on GPU fleets; a model never retrains itself mid-chat. ✘ D — a dropped packet would have broken message 4 then; the bot answered it correctly at the time.

Q10 · The receptionist who won't gossip 🧠 AI concept

Bruno's dental group subscribes to SmileDesk, an AI receptionist chatbot (SaaS). Patients have tried asking it for celebrity gossip, essay help, and stock tips; it always answers, politely: "I can only help with appointments and dental questions." Bruno knows the same famous model powers wide-open creative-writing apps elsewhere. Where does SmileDesk's on-topic discipline actually come from?

Answer: A. ✔ Vendors shape a general-purpose model with a system prompt — instructions injected ahead of every chat that the user never sees ("You are SmileDesk. Discuss only dental topics. Decline everything else politely."). It's Segment 1's configuration-not-custom-code rule in AI form: one multi-tenant model serves every SmileDesk customer, with per-product behavior set at the configuration layer. Build on the model API instead (PaaS) and your developers write that system prompt themselves. ✘ B — frontier-scale training costs tens of millions in GPU time (Session 1); an app vendor configures an existing model, it doesn't rebuild one — and the bot clearly processed the gossip request well enough to decline it. ✘ C — firewalls filter traffic by address and port, not conversational meaning; a blocked message would produce an error, not a polite refusal. ✘ D — the window limits how much text fits, never which topics are allowed; "any gossip?" is four tokens.
