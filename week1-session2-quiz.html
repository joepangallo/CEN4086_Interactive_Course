📝 Session 2 Quiz — SaaS & PaaS

Ten workplace scenarios on SaaS and PaaS — the last two test the session's AI concept. Pick the best answer for each; you can change your answer as many times as you like before submitting. Counts toward the syllabus "Exercises/Quizzes" grade (30%).

Q1 · Nine hundred florists, one database

Tessa starts as a junior engineer at BloomBase, a SaaS point-of-sale product used by 900 flower shops. On day one she discovers all 900 customers run on ONE shared application deployment and one database cluster, with a tenant_id column separating each shop's rows. Alarmed, she messages her manager: "Shouldn't every customer get their own copy of the app?" Her manager grins: "That would end the company." What is this architecture called, and why does BloomBase bet the business on it?

Q2 · The workflow that wouldn't bend

Grimaldi & Voss, a 60-lawyer firm, adopts a popular SaaS practice-management suite at $59/user/month. Six weeks in, the partners demand the intake screens be rebuilt to mirror the firm's bespoke 14-step conflict-check process, exactly as their old custom system did it. The vendor replies: configuration options exist — custom fields, templates, toggles — but changes to core workflow code ship to ALL customers or none, and a private fork is impossible. Which SaaS characteristic did the firm collide with?

Q3 · Run the numbers, Ren

Ren must present the 3-year cost of email + docs for Solstice Outfitters' 150 staff. Option 1, SaaS suite: $11/user/month. Option 2, on-prem: $310/user perpetual licenses, $14,000 in server hardware, plus a support contract averaging 20% of total license cost per year. Using 36 months and the method from today's calculator, which option is cheaper, and by roughly how much?

Q4 · The exit door

After a bruising migration OUT of a proprietary CRM — the data export alone cost $18,000 in consulting fees and arrived as half-corrupted CSVs — CTO Amara writes one non-negotiable rule for the next SaaS purchase: the product must be built on open-source software with standard data formats, so her company could self-host the same software or switch hosts if the vendor relationship sours. What is Amara demanding?

Q5 · Push and pray no more

Deshawn's four-developer team at Kettle & Crumb built the bakery chain's custom online-ordering app. Today, every release means a Saturday of manually copying files onto a rented virtual machine, editing configs by hand, and praying. They employ no sysadmin and never want to. They move to a service where git push automatically triggers dependency install, tests, a zero-downtime rollout, and traffic-based scaling. What did they adopt — and what do they still manage afterward?

Q6 · The golden handcuffs

Two years after building its logistics app on the CloudNimbus PaaS, Vantage Freight receives a renewal quote 40% higher. Shopping around, the CTO discovers the app uses CloudNimbus's proprietary message-queue service, its non-standard database API, and its custom authentication SDK; engineers estimate seven months of rewriting to run anywhere else. The account rep, reviewing the same architecture, does not seem worried about losing the deal. What is this situation called, and what would have reduced it?

Q7 · The server in the closet retires

Harbor Lane Books' Exchange email server (installed 2016) shares the famous closet with everything else. It demands monthly security patches, needs a $9,000 hardware refresh this year, and crashed during the last two author-event weekends. Ops manager Coral proposes SaaS email at $6/user/month for all 240 staff. The owner asks the right question: "Tell me exactly what stops being OUR problem — and what doesn't." What is the accurate answer?

Q8 · Three quotes on the whiteboard

Nadia's six-person startup is building a custom subscription-box web app — the code IS the business. They have zero infrastructure staff and $40,000 of runway. On the whiteboard: (1) buy two servers for the office closet; (2) rent IaaS VMs and self-manage OS, runtime, and deploys; (3) adopt an established SaaS storefront that allows no custom code; (4) deploy their own code on a PaaS with automated build, deploy, and scaling. Which option fits BEST, and why?

Q9 · The goldfish support agent 🧠 AI concept

Leena manages support at VellumWorks, which licenses an AI chat agent (SaaS). A customer spends 90 minutes troubleshooting a printer; in message 4 she gave her order number and printer model. After an hour of pasted log files, the bot cheerfully asks: "Could you tell me your printer model?" The customer is livid, and Leena must explain to her boss what technically happened. What's the accurate explanation?

Q10 · The receptionist who won't gossip 🧠 AI concept

Bruno's dental group subscribes to SmileDesk, an AI receptionist chatbot (SaaS). Patients have tried asking it for celebrity gossip, essay help, and stock tips; it always answers, politely: "I can only help with appointments and dental questions." Bruno knows the same famous model powers wide-open creative-writing apps elsewhere. Where does SmileDesk's on-topic discipline actually come from?
