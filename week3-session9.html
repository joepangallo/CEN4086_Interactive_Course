1 What Can Go Wrong — and What Every Hour Costs

The threat list is longer than "hurricane"

Today's session opens with a truth Florida students grasp faster than most: disasters happen. But the threat list deliberately reaches past weather, because the threats that actually take companies down are usually smaller and closer: server failure (power supplies and disks die on ordinary Tuesdays), network failure (a backhoe finds the fiber; an ISP has a bad morning), database failure or corruption (the data is "there" but wrong — often nastier than data that's gone), and even phone-system failure — if customers can't reach you, you're down in every way that matters to them. Add the modern chart-toppers: human error (a wrong command deleting the wrong thing — consistently a leading cause of major outages) and ransomware, which is a disaster engineered on purpose. A plan that only imagines hurricanes will be defeated by a typo.

Business continuity vs disaster recovery — two disciplines, one goal

These terms get blurred in meetings; don't let them blur on your exam. Disaster recovery (DR) is the technology discipline: restoring systems, data, and connectivity after an event — the runbooks, replicas, and backups. Business continuity (BC) is the business discipline: keeping the organization operating through the event — people, processes, communications, suppliers, payroll, and phones, of which IT recovery is one piece. A bookstore whose servers failed over beautifully but whose staff don't know whether to come to work, whose customers hear silence, and whose registers have no fallback procedure has DR without BC — and is still, functionally, closed. Misconception to kill: "DR is an IT problem." The recovery is IT's; deciding what must survive, in what order, at what cost, is a business decision made by owners — which is why your capstone DR plan opens with business priorities, not server names.

Measuring the blast radius: business impact analysis

Before buying any protection, quantify what an outage costs: lost revenue per hour, idled payroll, recovery labor, contractual penalties, and the slow poison of reputation. That exercise — the business impact analysis (BIA) — produces the dollars-per-hour figure that justifies (or forbids) every DR expense downstream. Compute one now.

2 RTO & RPO: The Two Numbers That Design Your DR

Two questions, two clocks

Every DR conversation reduces to two questions asked of the business, not of IT. Question one: "How long can we afford to be down?" The answer is the RTO — Recovery Time Objective — the clock that starts at the failure and stops when service is restored. It drives your compute and failover architecture: the shorter the RTO, the more standby infrastructure must already exist, warmed and waiting. Question two: "How much data can we afford to lose?" The answer is the RPO — Recovery Point Objective — measured backward from the failure to your last good copy. It drives your backup and replication frequency: nightly backups mean a worst-case RPO of 24 hours; continuous replication buys an RPO of seconds. Memory hook: RTO is about Time offline; RPO is about the Point you restore to. They are independent — a business can tolerate a day of downtime but almost no data loss (a law firm), or the reverse (a news site).

The brutal economics: each 10× tightening of either number multiplies cost, because you graduate through four canonical architecture tiers. Backup & restore — cheap nightly copies to cloud storage, rebuild when disaster hits (RTO: a day or more, RPO: up to 24h). Pilot light — data replicates continuously to a minimal core that idles in the cloud; on disaster you "turn up the flame" and build the full environment around it (RTO: hours, RPO: minutes). Warm standby — a scaled-down but fully functional copy runs at all times; failover = scale up and switch traffic (RTO: minutes, RPO: seconds-to-minutes). Multi-site active-active — two or more full sites serve live traffic simultaneously; losing one is a non-event users barely notice (RTO ≈ 0, RPO ≈ 0, and you pay for it every single month). Misconception: "we have backups, so we have DR." Backups answer RPO only — a backup you need 3 days to restore onto hardware you haven't bought yet is a 72-hour RTO wearing a disguise.

3 SLAs, Backup Strategy & Writing the Plan Itself

Reading an SLA like a lawyer (and a mathematician)

A Service Level Agreement is the provider's written promise: an uptime percentage, definitions of what counts as "down," exclusions (scheduled maintenance often doesn't count), how you must measure and report, and the remedy. Read that remedy twice: it is almost always service credits — a discount on next month's bill — not compensation for your lost revenue. An SLA is a refund policy, not insurance. The percentages also deserve arithmetic respect: each added "nine" cuts allowed downtime by 10× and raises the provider's engineering cost accordingly. And here is the trap that catches real architects: availabilities multiply through dependencies. If your app needs an auth service at 99.9% AND a payment API at 99.9%, your best case is 99.9% × 99.9% ≈ 99.8% — chaining two three-nine services quietly doubles your expected downtime, assuming they even fail independently. You cannot promise your customers more nines than the weakest chain of things you depend on.

Backup strategy: the 3-2-1 rule and the restore test

Backup taxonomy first: a full backup copies everything (slow to make, fast to restore); an incremental copies only changes since the last backup of any kind (fast to make, but restoring replays a chain); a differential copies changes since the last full (a middle path). Whatever the mix, the gold standard is the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies of your data, on 2 different types of media or systems, with 1 copy off-site — and in the ransomware era, make that off-site copy offline, immutable, or versioned, because malware that encrypts your disk will cheerfully encrypt a mounted backup drive and let a sync service replicate the damage everywhere. Two more truths the industry keeps relearning: snapshots are not backups (they live on the same storage array they'd need to survive), and an untested backup is a hope, not a plan — schedule restore drills, because backup jobs fail silently and you want to discover that on a calm Tuesday, not during the fire.

The DR plan document — what's actually in it

Here is today's DR-plan template, its skeleton in one breath — and Lab C has you draft one tonight. (1) Scope & targets: which system, its RTO/RPO, its dependencies. (2) Activation: who declares a disaster, on what criteria, and the call tree with backups for every name. (3) Recovery runbook: numbered, command-level steps a stressed person can follow at 3 a.m. — assume the author is unavailable. (4) Data restore & verification: where the copies live, how far back, and the query that proves the restored data is sane. (5) Communications: what staff, customers, and partners are told, by whom, how often — silence is its own outage. (6) Fallbacks & escalation when a step fails. (7) Test schedule & revision log: a plan nobody has rehearsed is fiction with a header. Note how little of this is technology: DR plans are mostly decisions made in advance, which is the entire point — decisions are cheap before the disaster and expensive during it.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · AI Reliability — designing for the day the model is down

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, while mastering DR and business continuity (Objective 7 applied), you learn to treat a model API as what it is — a third-party dependency with its own SLA, outages, rate limits, and silent quality decay — and to design the fallback chain before the day you need it.

The guardian side: the most valuable disaster is the one that never happens, and prediction is machine learning's home turf. Cloud providers run models on disk telemetry (SMART data) to forecast drive failures days ahead, migrating data off the doomed hardware before it dies — a disaster silently canceled. Anomaly-detection models watch metrics and traffic for the weird precursors of outages (memory creeping, latency drifting, a traffic pattern that preceded last month's incident) and page a human while the problem is still small. AIOps platforms triage floods of alerts into the three that matter. In DR terms, AI shortens the time-to-detect — and every minute of earlier detection is a minute off your effective RTO.

The dependency side — new for your generation of architects: every AI feature you add is a dependency with its own availability, and model APIs have real outages, sometimes hours long, as status pages cheerfully document. If Harbor Lane's checkout page waits on a recommendation model, then when that API is down, checkout is down — you have chained your revenue to someone else's SLA (remember the multiplication from Segment 3). The pattern that fixes it is graceful degradation: cache yesterday's recommendations, hide the widget on timeout, fall back to "bestsellers this week," and never let a nice-to-have block a must-have. Ask of every AI feature what you now ask of every system: what happens when it is down at our busiest hour — and what is the fallback?

The concept: the model API is a dependency — with moods

Most companies never run their own models: the "AI feature" is an HTTPS call to a third-party inference API. That makes it a dependency with everything Segment 3 taught you to read — its own SLA (with the usual service-credit remedy), real outages that status pages document in multi-hour increments, rate limits that return 429 errors precisely when traffic spikes (your busiest hour is everyone else's too), version deprecations on the vendor's schedule, and price changes you don't control. Rule one is mechanical: wrap every AI call in a timeout and a spending budget, so a slow model can't hang your page and a runaway loop can't burn a month of tokens in an afternoon.

Rule two is this session's tier thinking, shrunk from hours to milliseconds: design the fallback chain before launch. For Harbor Lane's recommendation widget: (1) the primary model answers fresh, under a strict timeout; (2) on error or timeout, serve cached recent recommendations — slightly stale beats absent; (3) beneath that, a simple rules-based stand-in ("bestsellers this week"); (4) at the bottom, a feature flag hides the widget entirely, because a nice-to-have must never block checkout. That ladder is graceful degradation — each tier trades a little quality to keep the page alive.

The SLA multiplication from Segment 3 applies without mercy: a 99.9% site whose feature also needs a 99.5% model API can honestly promise at most 0.999 × 0.995 ≈ 99.4% for that feature — roughly 52 hours a year of expected AI downtime against the ~9 your site alone would allow. And the sneakiest failure never pages anyone: model drift, the silent quality regression. The world changes while the model doesn't (or a quiet vendor swap changes the model), and answers get gradually worse over months with every uptime monitor solid green — an outage of correctness rather than availability. The counter is quality monitoring: score samples of output, watch "was this helpful?" clicks, and alarm on trend. Fittingly, AI also plays guardian here — anomaly detection over your own metrics warns before the crash, subtracting minutes from your effective RTO.

Misconception to kill: "if our servers are up, our AI feature is up." Your dashboard can glow green while the feature is degraded (rate-limited), dead (provider outage), or lying (drift). Monitor the dependency and the quality, not just your own uptime — and rehearse the day the model is down exactly the way you just rehearsed the hurricane.

Q1 · The 4:45 p.m. encryption

Solstice Dental Partners backs up its patient-scheduling database every night at 11:00 p.m. On Thursday at 4:45 p.m., ransomware encrypts the live database. The backups are clean and restore perfectly by Friday morning. Office manager Deb celebrates — until the schedulers discover every appointment, payment, and note entered since Wednesday night is simply gone. Which statement correctly frames what happened?

Answer: B. ✔ Count backward from the 4:45 p.m. failure to the last good copy (11:00 p.m. Wednesday): ~17.75 hours of work vanished. That window is the recovery point — nightly backups mathematically guarantee an RPO of up to 24 hours, and Thursday afternoon is nearly the worst case. If losing a day of appointments is unacceptable, the business must buy a tighter RPO: hourly incrementals or continuous replication. ✘ A swaps the definitions — RTO measures downtime duration (theirs was decent: down Thursday evening, up Friday morning); data loss is RPO's department. ✘ C confuses "backup worked" with "no data lost" — the restore succeeded to a point 18 hours old. ✘ D — SLAs remedy the provider's uptime with service credits; no SLA re-types your lost appointments.

Q2 · Thirty minutes, one minute

Verdant Vines sells wine subscriptions online at roughly $9,000/hour. After a painful outage last year, the board sets hard targets for the ordering system: no more than 15 minutes of downtime per incident, and no more than 1 minute of lost transactions. CTO Rafael must pick the least expensive DR architecture that honestly meets both numbers. Which does he choose?

Answer: C. ✔ Work the two clocks. RPO of 1 minute demands continuous replication — backups of any nightly flavor are disqualified instantly. RTO of 15 minutes means there is no time to build an environment; something functional must already be running, needing only scale-up and a traffic switch. That is warm standby's exact definition, at roughly half the cost of running two full live sites. ✘ A fails both clocks catastrophically (24h RPO, day-plus RTO). ✘ B satisfies the RPO but not the RTO — "turning up the flame" (provisioning the full stack around the pilot light) takes hours, not minutes. ✘ D would meet the targets with room to spare, but at ~2× warm standby's cost the board didn't ask for; "least expensive architecture that meets the targets" is the entire discipline of DR economics.

Q3 · Six hours and a coupon

Cobalt Web Hosting promises Kettle & Crumb Bakery "99.9% uptime" for its online-ordering site. In March alone, the site is down six hours during a storage migration gone wrong, costing the bakery about $4,000 in lost orders. Owner Mei re-reads the contract to see what she is owed. What is the accurate reading of the situation?

Answer: A. ✔ Both halves matter. The math: 0.1% of a ~730-hour month ≈ 43.8 minutes, so a six-hour outage obliterates a monthly 99.9% commitment. The law: SLA remedies are almost universally service credits — a discount on next month's hosting bill, likely a few dollars — while Mei's $4,000 of lost orders is her own loss to absorb or insure. This is the "refund policy, not insurance" lesson, and it is why businesses with real revenue-per-hour build their own DR rather than leaning on a vendor's percentage. ✘ B misapplies the yearly figure (8.76 hours per year) to a month. ✘ C — no contract prevents outages; SLAs price them. ✘ D inverts typical contracts: scheduled maintenance is usually excluded from downtime calculations, not double-counted — read the exclusions section first.

Q4 · The nines that multiplied

Architect Imani is designing a loan-application portal that must call three things to serve a single request: the company's own infrastructure (SLA 99.95%), an identity provider (99.9%), and a credit-check API (99.9%). Marketing wants to advertise "99.9% availability" on the portal's landing page. Imani runs the numbers. What should she report?

Answer: D. ✔ A request that requires all three components succeeds only when all three are up; independent availabilities therefore multiply: 0.9995 × 0.999 × 0.999 = 0.99750…, or ~99.75% — about 21.9 hours of expected downtime per year versus the 8.76 the marketing claim implies. Imani can only advertise 99.9% by engineering for it: redundant credit-check vendors, cached identity sessions, graceful degradation — the same fallback thinking today's AI Spotlight applied to model APIs. ✘ A: averaging is meaningless — a chain is not improved by its strongest link. ✘ B: the minimum is an upper bound fantasy; the chain is strictly worse than its weakest link because failures don't politely coordinate. ✘ C is how companies end up apologizing on social media for "a vendor issue" their own architecture chose to depend on.

Q5 · Three copies that were one copy

Okeechobee Outfitters believes it takes backups seriously: the production database lives on the office NAS, a nightly backup job writes to a second folder on the same NAS, and a consumer sync service mirrors the whole NAS to the cloud in real time. Ransomware detonates on a Tuesday: it encrypts the NAS — both folders — and the sync service faithfully replicates the encrypted files to the cloud within minutes. Every "backup" is gone. Which principle did their design violate?

Answer: C. ✔ They had three copies in name and one failure domain in fact: two folders shared one NAS (one media/system), and the "off-site" copy was a live mirror that propagates every change — including encryption — instantly. Sync answers the question "make there look like here"; backup answers "return me to yesterday." The 3-2-1 fix: nightly copies to a second system type, plus an off-site copy that is versioned or immutable so Tuesday's poison can't overwrite Monday's truth. ✘ A counts copies without counting failure domains — the rule's entire point. ✘ B: RAID survives disk hardware failure; it dutifully mirrors ransomware's writes like any other writes. ✘ D: RTO governs how fast you restore; their problem was having nothing clean to restore from — an RPO/architecture failure.

Q6 · The servers survived; the business didn't

A hurricane closes Gulfhaven Escrow's headquarters for two weeks. Their IT, migrated to the cloud last year, performs flawlessly — every system reachable from anywhere. Yet the company nearly collapses anyway: employees don't know whether to work or where; the main phone number rings into a dead PBX in the flooded building; clients hear nothing for days and start moving deals to competitors; nobody is authorized to sign payroll because both signers evacuated without a protocol. What did Gulfhaven have, and what did it lack?

Answer: A. ✔ This is the cleanest possible dissection of the two disciplines. DR — restoring systems, data, connectivity — succeeded completely; the technology was never the problem. What failed was everything BC covers and DR doesn't: a work-from-where policy, call trees and staff communication, customer messaging cadence, phone-system failover, and pre-delegated signing authority. BC treats IT recovery as one piece of a larger plan for keeping the organization alive. ✘ B inverts the facts — the systems ran; the business stalled. ✘ C slanders a migration that performed flawlessly; blaming the one component that worked is how post-mortems learn nothing. ✘ D is fatalism: every listed failure (phones, comms, authority) is cheap to fix in advance — which is why your D3 plan includes a communications section, not just runbooks.

Q7 · The backup that had been failing since May

Pinecrest Dental's server dies in October. No panic: nightly backups have run for years, and the green "job complete" light was on just last night. During the restore, the truth surfaces: a configuration change in May made the job silently back up an empty directory. Five months of "successful" backups contain nothing. The practice loses its schedule, billing, and chart annotations back to spring. What is the core lesson for the post-mortem?

Answer: D. ✔ The failure wasn't frequency, hardware, or vendor — it was verification. Backup jobs are software; software fails quietly, and a green checkmark measures "the job ran," not "the data is restorable." The professional counters: scheduled restore tests (actually rebuild a system from backup onto scratch hardware or a VM — Session 7 makes this nearly free), plus monitoring that alerts when backup size or file count drops off a cliff, as five months of empty archives would have instantly. "You don't have a backup until you've restored one." ✘ A doubles the frequency of backing up nothing. ✘ B — no SLA insures the customer's own misconfigured job. ✘ C repeats Session 8's classic confusion: RAID protects against disk failure while faithfully mirroring every logical mistake, deletion, and corruption — it is redundancy, not history.

Q8 · Multi-site everything!

After reading a horror story about a competitor's week-long outage, the CFO of Marlin & Reef Insurance orders IT to protect every system — the claims platform, the customer portal, the internal wiki, the cafeteria menu app — with multi-site active-active DR "so we're never down again." IT lead Santos estimates this at 10× the current infrastructure budget. What should Santos propose instead?

Answer: B. ✔ This is the whole session in one decision. DR spending is justified by the BIA's dollars-per-hour, and those dollars differ by orders of magnitude across systems: claims processing might cost $80k/hour down (buy multi-site or warm standby), while the cafeteria menu costs approximately nothing (nightly backup, restore whenever). Matching tier to impact delivers the CFO's real goal — the business never meaningfully down — at perhaps a fifth of the uniform price. Your Lab A step 6 table for Harbor Lane is precisely this exercise. ✘ A spends 10× to over-protect trivia; "defensible" means matched to impact, not maximal. ✘ C is the opposite gamble, refuted by the competitor's week-long lesson. ✘ D confuses age with criticality — the dustiest system in the building may be the payroll engine.

Q9 · The nine that wasn't there 🧠 AI concept

Nadir's company runs its storefront at a measured 99.9% availability. The new AI gift-finder feature calls an outside model API whose SLA is 99.5%. Marketing drafts a launch banner: "AI Gift-Finder — available 99.9% of the time, guaranteed." Nadir is asked to approve the claim. What should he say?

Answer: D. ✔ The gift-finder answers only when site AND model are up, so independent availabilities multiply: 0.999 × 0.995 = 0.994005 ≈ 99.4%. The missing 0.5995% of 8,760 hours is ≈ 52.5 hours a year — six times the ~8.8 hours customers picture at "99.9%." To advertise more nines than your weakest dependency, engineer around it: a cache tier and a rules-based fallback keep the widget useful while the API is not (and recall the vendor's SLA remedy is service credits, not Nadir's lost holiday sales). ✘ A — where the code runs doesn't revive a dead dependency; the call still fails on your beautiful, healthy servers. ✘ B — Q4 already buried this: chained dependencies always multiply downward. ✘ C — averaging is the seductive wrong math; a chain is never improved by its stronger link.

Q10 · Four ways down, gracefully 🧠 AI concept

Hardening Harbor Lane's recommendation widget before the holiday rush, developer Sana must specify exactly what the widget does at each failure tier of its model-API dependency. Which specification implements graceful degradation correctly?

Answer: B. ✔ Each tier trades a little quality for guaranteed availability, and the ladder ends in honest absence instead of a broken page: stale-but-personal beats generic, generic beats blank, blank beats blocking checkout. The strict timeout on tier 1 matters as much as the tiers themselves — without it, a hanging API stalls the page and no fallback ever fires. This is RTO thinking shrunk to milliseconds: decide before the failure what happens during it. ✘ A — an infinite retry loop turns one outage into a self-inflicted flood against a struggling vendor while the customer watches a spinner. ✘ C inverts the BIA catastrophically: a nice-to-have takes down the revenue engine; no impact analysis on earth supports that trade. ✘ D over-corrects — fallback design exists precisely to capture a feature's value without inheriting all of its downtime.
