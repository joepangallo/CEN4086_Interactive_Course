1 The Identity Problem & How SSO Works

One employee, twenty-three passwords

Identity management is the set of processes and technology an organization uses to answer two questions for every system, every second: who is this user? (authentication) and what are they allowed to do? (authorization). Before cloud identity, every application answered those questions alone. Picture Harbor Lane Books' 240 employees: a store associate touches the POS register, email, the scheduling app, the HR portal, and the inventory system — five separate username/password databases, five places to forget a password, five databases a thief could steal. Multiply by 240 people and the costs get very real: password fatigue drives reuse, so one phished credential unlocks many systems; industry surveys consistently attribute somewhere between a fifth and half of all help-desk tickets to password resets, at tens of dollars of staff time each; and when someone quits, IT must remember every system that ever knew them. This is the core challenge that Identity as a Service exists to solve: move the "who are you?" function out of every individual app and into one professionally run cloud service.

Single sign-on (SSO), defined

SSO means a user authenticates once to a trusted identity provider (IdP), and every connected application (each a service provider) accepts the IdP's cryptographically signed token instead of running its own login. Why it matters: users juggle one strong credential instead of dozens; security teams get one choke point to enforce MFA, lockouts, and instant revocation; and the apps themselves stop storing passwords entirely, which shrinks the attack surface dramatically. You already live this: log into your university portal once, and email, the LMS, and the library all open without new logins.

Common misconception: "SSO means one password to lose — that's weaker!" In practice it's the opposite: SSO concentrates defense where it's strongest. The IdP does MFA, anomaly detection, and a one-click kill switch; the alternative is 23 weak passwords scattered across 23 amateur login systems. The honest trade-off is different: if the IdP goes down, many doors stick at once — which is why Objective 5 says appraise IDaaS solutions: uptime SLA and outage history are evaluation criteria, not fine print.

2 Federation, OpenID Connect & Multi-Factor Authentication

Federated identity: trust that crosses company lines

SSO inside one company is powerful; federated identity management extends it across organizations. Federation means independent domains agree to trust each other's identity assertions: Harbor Lane's payroll is processed by a completely separate company, but employees reach the payroll site using Harbor Lane's own IdP — the payroll firm honors CloudGate's signed tokens and never stores a single Harbor Lane password. Consumer-scale federation is the "Sign in with Google / Apple" button from Poll 2: the recipe site outsources its entire login problem to an IdP with a thousand-person security team.

Three protocol names you must be able to place: SAML is the older XML-based enterprise federation standard (still everywhere in corporate apps). OAuth 2.0 is a protocol for delegated authorization — "this app may read my calendar" — not for proving who you are. OpenID Connect (OIDC) adds the identity layer on top of OAuth 2.0 and is the modern descendant of the original OpenID standard; it's what actually powers "Sign in with Google" today. Common misconception: "OAuth logs you in." By itself it doesn't — OAuth grants an app access to resources; OIDC is what asserts who you are. Interviewers love that distinction.

Multi-factor authentication: know · have · are

Passwords are something you know — and the knowable can be phished, guessed, or bought in bulk from a breach dump. MFA requires proof from different categories: something you know (password, PIN), something you have (a phone with an authenticator app, a hardware security key), something you are (fingerprint, face). Real two-factor means two different categories — password + PIN is still just two "knows," one category, one phishing email away from gone. Microsoft's much-quoted research finding is that MFA blocks the overwhelming majority (~99%) of automated account-compromise attacks. The factor ladder matters too: SMS codes are the weakest "have" (SIM-swap attacks steal your number), TOTP app codes are stronger, and FIDO2 security keys / passkeys are strongest because they're origin-bound: a fake login page on the wrong domain simply gets no answer, so real-time phishing relays fail.

3 The Account Lifecycle & the IDaaS Market

Provisioning & deprovisioning: joiner, mover, leaver

Provisioning is creating accounts and granting entitlements when someone joins or changes roles; deprovisioning is revoking everything when they leave. Identity professionals call the lifecycle JML — joiner, mover, leaver — and it's where identity programs quietly succeed or fail. Joiners are self-correcting: a new hire without email complains, loudly, until access appears. Leavers are the opposite: nobody complains about an account that should be dead but isn't. Those are orphaned accounts — live credentials with no living owner — and they are among the most common findings in real security audits, because ex-employee credentials are a classic breach entry point. Movers cause the subtle failure: promotions add access but old rights rarely get removed, so ten-year employees accumulate the permissions of every job they've ever held (privilege creep).

The IDaaS fix is to make the HR system the system of record: HR marks a person hired, promoted, or terminated, and connectors fan that single event out to every application — accounts created from a role template in minutes, entitlement bundles swapped on role change, and every door locked at once on termination. Misconception: "deprovisioning = delete the account." Best practice is disable immediately, delete later — you preserve files, mail, and audit trails while killing access instantly.

The IDaaS market — and how to appraise it

IDaaS is the identity function delivered as SaaS: a cloud directory, SSO, MFA, provisioning connectors, and reporting, sold per user per month. Objective 5 says appraise, so here is your evaluation checklist: protocol support (SAML and OIDC), size of the pre-built app catalog, HR-system connectors for JML automation, MFA options including phishing-resistant factors, uptime SLA and outage history, admin/reporting quality, and per-user price at your headcount. Meet the market:

Okta — the best-known independent IDaaS: huge pre-integrated app catalog (thousands of SaaS apps), strong JML workflow automation, vendor-neutral (plays equally well with Google or Microsoft shops). Priced per user/month, which a 240-employee retailer must budget line by line. Appraisal note: independence is the selling point — and, after publicized incidents, so is scrutinizing any vendor's breach transparency.
Microsoft Entra ID (the renamed Azure Active Directory) — the default gravity well for organizations already on Microsoft 365: identity is bundled into licenses many businesses own anyway, with deep Windows/Office integration and conditional-access policies (the risk-based rules in today's AI concept section). Appraisal note: superb value if you live in Microsoft's world; less neutral outside it.
Google Cloud Identity — identity from the Google Workspace side: if Harbor Lane ran Gmail/Docs, every employee already has a Google-managed identity that can SSO into third-party apps via SAML/OIDC. Free tier exists; premium adds device management. Appraisal note: same story as Microsoft — strongest when it's the suite you already pay for.
Auth0 (owned by Okta) — developer-focused: login-as-an-API for the apps you build, with a generous free tier students can try. Also in the market: Ping Identity, JumpCloud, Cisco Duo (MFA-centric). Appraisal note: "workforce identity" (employees → apps, today's topic) and "customer identity" (your app's users) are different products — Harbor Lane needs both eventually: workforce IDaaS now, customer login for the web store in Week 4.

Mobile identity management

Identity management's last frontier is mobile, and the phone has become both the biggest identity risk and the strongest identity anchor. Risk: a lost, unlocked phone holds your email — the master key to every "reset my password" flow. Anchor: that same device is your "something you have" — push-approval MFA, TOTP apps, and passkeys all live there, and companies use MDM (mobile device management) to require screen locks and remotely wipe corporate data from lost devices. Misconception worth killing: "Face ID uploads my face to the company." No — device biometrics match against a template stored in the phone's secure hardware; what leaves the device is only a cryptographic "yes, it's really them." Your face never crosses the network. That design — biometric unlocks a local private key — is exactly how passkeys make phishing-proof login feel like a glance.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · Deepfakes vs. MFA — when AI attacks (and defends) identity

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, while appraising IDaaS for Objective 5, you learn how generative AI broke the oldest authentication assumption — being recognized — and how the same technology now guards the login from the other side.

The concept: for a century, "prove who you are" quietly leaned on recognition — a colleague knows your voice, a manager knows your face on a video call. Generative AI just broke that assumption. A deepfake is synthetic media produced by a model trained to imitate a specific real person, and the entry price has collapsed: current voice-cloning models need only seconds of sampled audio (a voicemail greeting, one TikTok) to produce a clone that fools family members, and face-generation models can animate a convincing video head from a handful of photos. Notice precisely what broke: not cryptography, not passwords — the "something you are" factor as judged by humans. A human ear on a phone line was never a verification protocol; it just used to be expensive to fool, and now it costs almost nothing.

The countermeasures come in two layers. First, liveness detection tries to prove a live human is present, not a replay or a render: challenge-response ("turn your head left," "read this random phrase" — a pre-rendered fake can't comply instantly), depth sensing (Face ID projects thousands of infrared dots to map 3-D facial structure; a flat screen has none), and artifact analysis hunting for the statistical fingerprints synthesis leaves behind. Second — and this is where IDaaS earns its subscription — AI plays defense through behavioral anomaly detection: the platform learns each user's normal (login hours, devices, geography, even typing cadence) and challenges whatever deviates — the famous impossible travel rule, a payroll page opened at 3 AM, a password typed in a stranger's rhythm. Security teams call this the emerging fourth factor: something you do. Behavior is continuous and hard to imitate at length, unlike a face on a screen. Even the humble CAPTCHA is caught in this arms race: AI now solves classic squiggly-letter tests better than humans do, so bot-vs-human proof has shifted toward invisible, risk-based checks (mouse paths, device fingerprints, interaction history) that only surface a puzzle when risk runs high.

The misconception to kill: "biometrics can't be faked." A biometric is not a secret — your face is public and your voice is recorded every time you leave a voicemail. It's a measurement, and measurements can be synthesized. What actually holds is narrower: a biometric checked locally, with liveness detection, unlocking a cryptographic key (exactly how passkeys work — Segment 3) stays strong, because an attacker needs your physical presence, not your likeness. A biometric judged by a human over a remote channel — a voice on a phone, a face in a video call — has been demoted to a vibe, and vibes now spoof at scale. That's why the real answer to deepfakes is procedural: out-of-band verification and step-up MFA for any request that moves money or credentials.

Identity is now an AI-versus-AI battlefield, and both sides matter for Objective 5. On defense, IDaaS platforms run machine-learning models on every single login: device fingerprint, network, geography, time of day, and typing cadence get compressed into a risk score. Low risk — Maya, usual laptop, usual store, Tuesday morning — sails through. Medium risk triggers a step-up: prove a second factor before continuing. High risk gets blocked outright, like the famous impossible travel pattern: a login from Tampa followed forty minutes later by the "same user" from another continent — no airplane explains that, but a stolen password does. This adaptive, risk-based authentication is why modern MFA doesn't have to nag you every five minutes: the model spends friction only where risk lives.

On offense, attackers use AI to write flawless phishing pages, run credential-stuffing bots that solve CAPTCHAs, and — the new frontier — clone voices and faces. In a widely reported 2024 Hong Kong case, an employee wired roughly US$25 million after a video call with what turned out to be deepfaked colleagues. Moral for your capstone: any factor an AI can imitate (a voice on the phone, a face on a video call) is weakening; factors bound to cryptography and origin (security keys, passkeys) are not imitable at a distance. High-stakes requests deserve out-of-band verification — call back on a known number.

Q1 · Reset o'clock

Priya runs the help desk at Bayside Credit Union: 900 staff, 27 applications, each with its own login. Last quarter 31% of her 4,800 tickets were password resets, and the bank estimates $30 of staff time per reset. The new CIO proposes an IDaaS platform with single sign-on, and the board asks Priya to name the PRIMARY operational benefit. What should she say?

Answer: C. ✔ Twenty-seven credential sets become one: the ~1,500 quarterly resets (≈$45,000 of staff time) shrink toward a fraction, and every security control — MFA, lockout thresholds, the termination kill switch — is enforced once at the IdP rather than replicated 27 times, badly. That's the operational case in one sentence. ✘ A — SSO relocates authentication to the IdP; users absolutely still authenticate, just once and under stronger controls. ✘ B is backwards and dangerous: the whole point is that apps stop keeping password databases and accept signed tokens instead. One giant shared password database would be a bigger prize for attackers, not a benefit. ✘ D oversells: phishing pressure drops (fewer credentials, central anomaly detection), but no control eliminates phishing — promising that to a board destroys credibility at the first incident.

Q2 · The charter partner

Gulf Coast Charters books fishing trips through BookingHarbor, a portal run by a completely separate company. The contract says Gulf Coast's 85 employees must reach BookingHarbor using their existing Gulf Coast logins, and BookingHarbor may never store a Gulf Coast password. BookingHarbor's engineer shrugs: "Standard setup, we do it weekly." What is being described?

Answer: A. ✔ Trust that crosses organizational lines is the definition of federation: Gulf Coast's IdP authenticates its own people and issues signed tokens (via SAML or OIDC); BookingHarbor verifies the signature and lets them in — no Gulf Coast password ever exists on BookingHarbor's side, exactly as the contract demands. ✘ B — multi-tenancy is one provider serving many customers on shared infrastructure (Session 2); sharing a user database between two companies is neither multi-tenancy nor a good idea. ✘ C — vaulting stored passwords is precisely what the contract forbids; federation exists so partners never need your secrets. ✘ D — deprovisioning is revoking access when people leave; deleting accounts after each booking would force constant re-provisioning and has nothing to do with how login trust works.

Q3 · The two-factor audit

Security auditor Nakia is reviewing authentication schemes proposed for Suncoast Dental's four systems. The office manager insists all four are "two-factor because there are two steps." Scheme 1: password + security question. Scheme 2: password + 4-digit PIN. Scheme 3: fingerprint + face scan. Scheme 4: password + a 6-digit code from an authenticator app on the employee's enrolled phone. Which scheme should Nakia certify as TRUE two-factor authentication?

Answer: D. ✔ True MFA requires factors from different categories. Scheme 4 combines something you KNOW (password) with something you HAVE (the TOTP code proves possession of the enrolled phone) — two categories, so one phishing email or one stolen phone alone isn't enough. ✘ A is two KNOWs: a security question is just a second, usually weaker, password ("first pet's name" is often on Instagram). ✘ B is also two KNOWs — both live in the victim's head and both fall to the same phishing page. ✘ C is two AREs: sounds exotic, but both factors are the same category; a scenario that defeats device biometrics tends to defeat both, and biometrics can't be rotated after compromise. Two steps ≠ two factors — the category math is what Nakia is paid to check.

Q4 · The ghost of Region 4

Whitfield Insurance terminated regional manager Dale in March. By August, a competitor was mysteriously undercutting Whitfield's renewal quotes in Region 4. Forensics found the answer: Dale's CRM account pulled 1,200 customer records in July — from his home IP. His email was disabled the day he left, but the CRM "wasn't on the termination checklist," and nobody owned the checklist anyway. What was the ROOT failure?

Answer: B. ✔ This is the orphaned-account horror story from Segment 3, beat for beat: leavers don't complain about access they shouldn't have, manual checklists always miss something, and five months later the miss is exfiltrating customer data. The fix is structural — HR's termination event automatically fans out to every connected app, with periodic access reviews to catch strays. ✘ A — entropy is irrelevant; Dale used his own valid, active credential. A 40-character password that should have been revoked is still a breach. ✘ C — SSO would have helped (one kill switch), but the root failure is the missing leaver process; SSO with no deprovisioning discipline still leaves direct-login accounts alive. ✘ D — TLS protects data in transit from eavesdroppers; it neither knows nor cares that the authenticated user was fired in March.

Q5 · The weekend project

Harbor Lane's only sysadmin, Owen, pushes back on the consultants: "IDaaS at $6 per user per month is about $17,000 a year for our 240 people. I can build a login system in a weekend — a password table and a form." The owner asks you for the STRONGEST argument in the IDaaS column. Which is it?

Answer: C. ✔ Owen's weekend project delivers a password table — none of the rest: no MFA, no federation to their SaaS apps, no JML automation, no 24/7 monitoring, and its security patches arrive only when Owen isn't fixing a POS terminal in Sarasota. $17k/yr against one breach or one Owen-shaped single point of failure is the whole argument. ✘ A misstates shared responsibility: Harbor Lane still owns role design, access policy, MFA rules, and offboarding discipline — the vendor runs the machinery, not the decisions. ✘ B — "always cheaper" ignores Owen's loaded hours, breach exposure, and opportunity cost; sometimes in-house wins, but never on "always." ✘ D — connectors, role bundles, and policies are genuine configuration work; pretending otherwise sets the project up to fail in week one.

Q6 · The perfect fake

A phishing email steers Harbor Lane staff to harbormail.example-support.net — a pixel-perfect proxy of the real login page that instantly relays whatever victims type to the genuine site: password AND the 6-digit authenticator code. Three employees fell for it and their accounts were taken over within seconds. Accountant Renata also clicked, also "logged in" — but the attackers got nothing usable. She signs in with a FIDO2 hardware security key. Why did her account survive?

Answer: A. ✔ Origin binding is the superpower: a FIDO2 key signs a challenge that includes the domain it's talking to. On example-support.net the signature is for the wrong origin — worthless at the real site — so a real-time relay has nothing to relay. This is why security keys and passkeys are called phishing-resistant while TOTP is merely phishing-slowing (her coworkers' 30-second codes were relayed comfortably inside their validity window). ✘ B — length is irrelevant here; the proxy captured passwords perfectly at any length. ✘ C — nothing rotates passwords; that garbles TOTP's mechanism, and TOTP demonstrably fell to this attack anyway. ✘ D — luck is not a control, and incident reports that credit luck get rewritten by the next incident.

Q7 · One button, no password

Jonah's two-person startup adds a "Sign in with Google" button to their book-club app specifically so they never have to store passwords. His cofounder panics during the demo: "Wait — so Google sends us the user's password and we check it?" What ACTUALLY happens when a user clicks that button?

Answer: B. ✔ This is OIDC — the identity layer on OAuth 2.0 from Segment 2. Google authenticates the user (password, MFA, risk checks), then hands the app a signed ID token: "this is jane@…, verified just now." Jonah's app validates the signature and trusts it — federation in one click, zero passwords stored. ✘ A — no provider ships password hashes to third parties; that would make every small app a leak point for Google credentials, the exact disease federation cures. ✘ C — classic scope confusion: proving identity grants NO resource access; reading Gmail or Drive requires separate OAuth scopes the user must explicitly approve on a consent screen. ✘ D — verification absolutely happened, just at Google, which does it better than a two-person startup ever could.

Q8 · Sixty elves for December

Harbor Lane hires 60 seasonal associates every November for the holiday rush. Last year onboarding took IT two full weeks of account tickets — and in January, an internal review found 19 seasonal accounts still active, one of which had processed a suspicious refund on December 26th. Four proposals are on the table for this year. As the consultant, which do you endorse?

Answer: D. ✔ Automated JML with role-based packages solves both stated failures: 60 accounts materialize from HR records in minutes (no two-week ticket queue), and expiry dates make January cleanup a non-event — access dies because the HR record says so, not because someone remembered. The suspicious-refund investigation also becomes possible: every action maps to a named person. ✘ A destroys accountability — with one shared login, the December 26th refund belongs to "everyone and no one," and you can't cut off one bad actor without locking out 59 others. ✘ B is privilege creep as a policy: temps inherit manager rights (refund overrides, payroll views) they must never have. ✘ C is the current failure mode with lamination; checklists owned by "whoever remembers" produced the 19 ghosts in the first place.

Q9 · The voice on line two 🧠 AI concept

Friday, 4:52 PM. Harbor Lane bookkeeper Nadia answers a call. It is unmistakably the owner's voice — his cadence, his little cough, the nickname only he uses for her — and caller ID shows his cell number. "A rare-book estate deal closes at 5:30. Wire the $18,000 deposit to this new account, and keep it quiet until Monday." Nadia went through this session's training. Which control ACTUALLY defeats this attack?

Answer: C. ✔ The attack lives entirely inside one inbound channel. Out-of-band verification exits that channel: Nadia originates a NEW call to a number she already trusts — which the attacker can't intercept — and a second-approver rule means even a flawless impersonation of one person isn't enough. The urgency and secrecy ("by 5:30," "keep it quiet") are social-engineering pressure designed to prevent exactly that callback. ✘ A — caller ID is trivially spoofable; it asserts a number, never a person. ✘ B is the trap this session exists to spring: her ear IS the attack surface — seconds of sampled audio built that voice, and recognition by humans was never a protocol. ✘ D is thin comfort: the dog's name is on Instagram, and interactive voice clones answer follow-up questions live. Personal trivia is research for the attacker, not authentication.
