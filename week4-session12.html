1 Why & What to Migrate: Drivers, Requirements & the 6 Rs

Business drivers: why companies finally move

Today's session opens where every real migration opens — not with technology, but with business drivers. The recurring five: stop buying hardware (CapEx→OpEx — Harbor Lane's owner said it word for word: "never buy another server"); scale for peaks (the author-event crashes); get disaster recovery that a server closet can never offer; reach staff and customers anywhere; and unlock capabilities — like AI recommendations — that only exist as cloud services. From drivers flow goals and requirements: measurable statements ("checkout p95 under 800 ms during author events," "RPO 15 minutes for orders") that later become your success criteria. The blunt truth: a migration without written success criteria cannot succeed or fail — it can only end, which is how projects drift for years. You also inventory application characteristics: age, vendor support, dependencies, data gravity, latency sensitivity, compliance constraints. A stateless web app with a modern stack migrates in a weekend; a latency-critical app hard-wired to a fax card does not.

The 6 Rs — the modern packaging of migration planning

Classic migration planning walks through migration components, requirements, and application analysis; the industry compressed those same decisions into a famous checklist — the 6 Rs. For every application you choose exactly one disposition. Rehost ("lift and shift"): move the VM as-is — fastest, least benefit, fine under deadline. Replatform ("lift, tinker, and shift"): small upgrades on the way — e.g., swap the hand-patched MySQL box for a managed database. Refactor: re-architect for cloud-native (microservices, autoscaling — your Session 10 skills) — most expensive, most payoff, reserved for apps where the business case is strong. Repurchase: drop the custom app and buy SaaS (goodbye home-grown CRM, hello subscription). Retire: the audit reveals nobody uses it — deleting is the cheapest migration of all (typically 10–20% of a portfolio!). Retain: keep on-prem for now — compliance, latency, or a pending contract says "not yet." Misconception: the 6 Rs are not a maturity ladder where Refactor is "best." The best R is the one whose cost matches the app's value — refactoring the cafeteria-menu app is malpractice.

2 Planning the Move: Waves, Data Protection, Lock-In & Budget

Waves, dependencies & protecting the data

Migrations ship in waves — small groups of applications moved together on a schedule — because dependencies impose an order. The iron rule: a system must migrate after the things it depends on. Identity moves early (everything logs in through it — Session 4); shared databases move before the apps that read them; the AI recommender moves last because it feeds on the web store's order stream. Wave 1 is deliberately low-risk (Poll 1): the team earns its scars on the file share. Meanwhile the most important part of today's playbook — protecting existing data — sets the safety rules: take a verified backup before anything moves; migrate a copy while the source keeps running (parallel run); validate the copy with row counts and checksums; and keep the old system intact until success criteria pass — the rollback path is the data you didn't touch. Teams skip these steps exactly once.

Vendors, lock-in & the budget

Vendor selection weighs services, pricing, region availability, compliance certifications, and support tiers — but the subtler question is vendor lock-in, and it's a gradient you choose, not a trap that happens to you. Every proprietary service you adopt (a provider-exclusive database, serverless framework, or event bus) buys real velocity today and raises the exit toll tomorrow; egress fees make moving large data out expensive; and multi-year commitments discount the bill while binding you contractually. Lock-in isn't sin — it's a price. Professionals measure it (the risk meter below), document an exit path, and take proprietary value with open eyes. On budget: migration costs run double for a while (old and new running in parallel), plus training, consulting, and data transfer. Reserved instances — committing 1–3 years for ~30–60% discounts — are powerful but belong at the end of the story: commit only after months of steady-state metrics tell you what you actually need. Reserving on day-one guesses is how you pay a discount price for the wrong servers.

3 Cutover Day, Training, Governance & Cloud Bursting

The weekend everything switches — and the people it happens to

Cutover is the choreographed window when traffic moves from old to new: announce a maintenance window and freeze changes; take one final verified backup; run the last data sync; validate the copy (row counts, checksums); repoint DNS; smoke-test the live system; watch dashboards through the first peaks (Session 11 skills on duty); and hold the rollback window open — the old servers stay untouched until success criteria pass. Decommissioning on day one is the classic irreversible mistake; the old system is your parachute, and you don't sell the parachute mid-jump. Notice what's absent from that list: heroics. A good cutover is boring by design — every step rehearsed, every step's undo known in advance.

Every serious migration plan devotes real attention to what technologists undervalue: training and change management. On Monday, 240 Harbor Lane employees meet new email, new logins, new POS reports. Untrained staff don't file tickets — they quietly revert: personal Dropbox accounts, spreadsheets emailed around, the old workflow resurrected in the shadows, and suddenly your migration "succeeded" while the business runs outside it. The countermeasures are managerial, not technical: train BEFORE cutover, name a champion per store, publish a who-to-call sheet, and measure adoption as a success criterion. Post-migration governance keeps the new world orderly — who may create resources, mandatory tagging, budget alerts (Session 11's guardrails) — because cloud sprawl is CapEx waste reincarnated as OpEx. Finally, cloud bursting: a hybrid pattern where baseline load runs on your steady infrastructure and overflow "bursts" to public cloud during spikes. For Harbor Lane's author events, bursting is the architectural punchline the owner has been waiting for since the Session 1 crash story.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · Adopting AI — it's a migration, run it like one

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, in the finale, while applying this session's migration playbook (Objective 8), you learn the track's last concept: responsible AI adoption — build vs. buy, governance, and the human in the loop.

The final AI concept is the one executives get wrong most expensively: adopting AI is a migration project, and the playbook you just learned applies verbatim. Write success criteria before choosing tools ("recommendations lift average basket 5%," not "we need AI"). Protect existing data — with a new question attached: what may leave the building at all? Customer PII, card numbers, and unreleased financials don't get pasted into a vendor's model any more than they get copied to a stranger's USB drive. Avoid lock-in with Segment 2's own moves: an abstraction layer so the model vendor can be swapped, exportable prompts and data, a second-source model actually tested. Roll out pilot → measure → scale in waves, never big-bang. And train the people and redesign the roles — Ray doesn't need a demo, he needs to co-design the workflow he'll operate, because staff who get AI imposed on them do what untrained migration users do: quietly revert.

Build vs. buy is a spectrum — 6-R thinking, reapplied. At Harbor Lane's size (240 people): consume AI-SaaS for commodity needs (support tooling with AI already inside — the Repurchase move); call a rented model's API and ground it in your own data with RAG (retrieval-augmented generation — the model reads your catalog and policies at question time) for the recommender, the replatform-shaped middle where most small-business value lives; fine-tune a rented model only when a measured pilot proves grounding isn't enough; and never train a model from scratch — that's a thousands-of-GPUs frontier-lab project (Session 1's math), not a bookstore's. The best R is still the one whose cost matches the app's value.

Governance is the cutover checklist for AI — the minimum kit has four parts. An acceptable-use policy: which data may be shared with which tools, written and trained, not implied. Human-review tiers by stakes: AI drafts a shelf-talker unsupervised; a human approves anything touching money, law, or a customer's record; refunds and hiring never ship on AI say-so alone. An AI use log: who used what, for what, verified how. And an incident path for the day an AI answer causes harm: who is told, what gets rolled back, what the postmortem records.

Common misconception: "adopting AI = picking the smartest model." Model choice is a fraction of the outcome. Adoption succeeds or fails on data readiness (is the catalog clean? are the policies written anywhere a model could read them?), process fit, and people — exactly the variables that decided every migration in this session. Q1 · Two apps, two Rs

Kellan is dispositioning two systems at Palmetto Insurance. App one: a stable claims-intake web app — the team will copy its VM to the cloud unchanged, same OS, same code, live by Friday. App two: the policy database — moving it too, but swapping the self-managed SQL install for the provider's managed database service (automatic backups and patching), with minor connection changes. Which 6-R labels apply, in order?

Answer: B. ✔ App one is the definitional rehost: same bits, new building — fastest path, least transformation. App two crosses the line into replatform: the app survives, but one component is upgraded to a managed service on the way ("tinker"). ✘ A — refactor means re-architecting the application itself (new design, not a copied VM), and repurchase means abandoning the app for SaaS; neither happened. ✘ C — retain keeps a system on-prem and retire deletes it; both apps are actively moving. ✘ D collapses the framework's most useful distinction: the moment you swap a component for a managed equivalent, you've bought different risk, cost, and benefit — which is exactly why the two labels exist.

Q2 · The audit's gift

Preparing Brightwater Dental's migration inventory, Suki discovers "ReferralTrack," an internally built app still running on its own server. Access logs show 4 logins in 2 years — all from one employee who retired in 2024. The vendor of record is Suki's own IT department; the data it holds was duplicated into the practice-management SaaS years ago. Migrating it would cost roughly $6,000 in labor. What does the 6-R framework say?

Answer: D. ✔ Zero active users, a retired sole user, data already duplicated elsewhere: ReferralTrack is portfolio dead weight, and retirement (with a data archive and a written decision record, in case an auditor asks) converts a $6,000 migration line into $0 plus recovered server capacity. Portfolio reviews routinely find 10–20% of apps in exactly this state. ✘ A spends real money faithfully preserving something nobody uses — waste, professionally executed. ✘ B is the same mistake at maximum price: refactoring is reserved for apps whose future value justifies it. ✘ C pays power, patching, and risk forever to avoid a decision; "just in case" is what the archived data is for.

Q3 · The night before

Tomorrow at 6 AM, Rakesh's team cuts the inventory system of Cypress Auto Parts over to the cloud. Tonight's checklist debate: one engineer argues the pre-migration backup is redundant "because the cloud copy IS the backup," and suggests skipping validation to save two hours. Per today's data-protection guidance, what must Rakesh insist on?

Answer: A. ✔ The three non-negotiables of protecting existing data: a verified backup taken while the source is still healthy (your state BEFORE anything could corrupt it), validation that the copy is complete and correct (row counts and checksums catch silent truncation), and the untouched old system as the rollback parachute. ✘ B confuses the migrating copy with a backup — if the migration process itself corrupts data, the "backup" carries the corruption. ✘ C runs production on unvalidated data all week; problems discovered Thursday have been compounding since Monday. ✘ D destroys the rollback path at the moment of maximum risk — the single most irreversible mistake in today's playbook. Confusion about the live copy is solved by the cutover plan, not by deletion.

Q4 · The golden handcuffs

Verra Logistics built its routing platform entirely on one provider's proprietary services: their exclusive event bus, their proprietary NoSQL database, their serverless framework. Development was fast. Three years later the provider raises prices 40%, and Verra's CTO discovers a competing cloud quote that would halve their bill — but engineering estimates 18 months and $2M to port the codebase, plus six-figure egress fees to move 200 TB out. What concept does this illustrate, and what was the sound approach at build time?

Answer: C. ✔ This is lock-in economics stated honestly: Verra's velocity was genuinely purchased by those proprietary services — and so was the $2M exit toll and the egress bill. The professional posture isn't abstinence; it's pricing the exit while you still have leverage: wrap proprietary APIs behind your own interfaces (Session 10's loose coupling, again), rehearse data export, and negotiate with a credible walk-away option. ✘ A — the invoice is accurate; portability was traded away in the architecture, not the billing system. ✘ B — every one of those dependencies was a design decision; "luck" is what unexamined decisions get renamed to. ✘ D overcorrects: for many workloads the proprietary service IS the right call — knowingly.

Q5 · The eager commitment

Day one of Meridian Media's migration, the CFO sees the pricing page: 3-year reserved instances cost 55% less than on-demand. "Lock in everything now," she says, "why would we pay full price?" The cloud architect, Ines, urges waiting 4–6 months. Who's right, and why?

Answer: B. ✔ Reserved pricing rewards predictability you can prove. During migration, instance types get resized (remember Session 11's 6%-utilized database?), apps get replatformed, and some get retired — day-one reservations freeze those guesses at 2026 prices for 3 years. The playbook: run on-demand, let monitoring reveal the stable baseline, then reserve THAT baseline and keep spiky remainder on-demand — the discount without the handcuffs. ✘ A treats a discount as free money; a 55% discount on the wrong servers is a 45% overpayment with a contract. ✘ C — for proven-stable workloads, reservations are excellent economics, not a scam. ✘ D reserves the mistake in its maximum size — over-provisioning with a loyalty oath.

Q6 · Eleven quiet months

Harbor Lane's web store runs comfortably on its baseline infrastructure 11 months of the year. But four times annually, author-event ticket drops multiply traffic 15× for six hours — the exact spikes that crashed the old server closet. The architecture team wants baseline costs to stay flat while spikes are absorbed automatically by temporary public-cloud capacity that vanishes afterward. What is this pattern called?

Answer: A. ✔ Cloud bursting is today's hybrid finale: steady base load on owned/steady infrastructure, elastic overflow rented for the peak — Harbor Lane pays for 15× capacity roughly 24 hours a year instead of 8,760. It's the direct architectural answer to the Session 1 crash story. ✘ B is a disaster-recovery posture (Session 9): standby replaces a FAILED site; bursting supplements a HEALTHY one that's merely overwhelmed. ✘ C hits the scale-up ceiling — no single resize absorbs 15×, and you'd pay for the big box year-round or eat a resize outage per event. ✘ D solves a different shape entirely: one huge computation chopped into pieces, not millions of small customer requests arriving at once.

Q7 · The migration that "worked"

Six weeks after Coquina Resorts' flawless technical migration — zero data loss, dashboards green — the CIO finds reservations staff at three properties running the old workflow through personal Dropbox accounts and emailed spreadsheets, because "the new system is confusing and nobody showed us." Bookings data is now fragmenting outside the migrated platform. What did the migration plan fail to include, per today's playbook?

Answer: D. ✔ This is the people-side failure today's session warns about: untrained users don't file tickets, they route around the system — and shadow IT (personal Dropbox holding guest data!) is now a security and compliance incident on top of an adoption one. Training before cutover, local champions, a visible help path, and adoption metrics belong in the plan with the same weight as backups. ✘ A misreads "confusing" as "slow" — speed doesn't teach workflows. ✘ B attacks the symptom; blocked from Dropbox, staff will find USB sticks — the unmet need remains. ✘ C polishes the part that already succeeded; no amount of cutover-weekend engineering teaches a reservations clerk the new screen on Monday.

Q8 · Define "done" before you jump

At the go/no-go meeting for Sandpiper Outfitters' store migration, the project sponsor asks, "How will we know Sunday whether to keep the new system or roll back?" The room offers four answers. Per today's success-criteria guidance, which one should the sponsor accept?

Answer: C. ✔ Success criteria must be measurable, agreed in advance, and wired to a rollback trigger — that's what converts Sunday from an argument into a checklist. Every threshold traces to a business requirement, and the rollback decision becomes automatic rather than political (nobody wants to be the person who "killed the migration" on vibes at 11 PM). ✘ A is unfalsifiable — instincts at hour 30 of a cutover weekend are noise. ✘ B measures complaint volume, which lags real damage (Session 11: customers-as-monitoring), and silence overnight proves nothing. ✘ D inverts the safety model: powering off the old system is the LAST step, gated by criteria like C — done right, it happens days later, after the parachute is provably unneeded.

Q9 · The 240-person question 🧠 AI concept

Cypress Trading Co. — a 240-person Gulf Coast retailer, remarkably like a certain bookstore chain — wants an AI assistant that answers product and policy questions using the company's own catalog and return rules. Four proposals land on CEO Adaora's desk. Applying the same cost-matches-value judgment as the 6 Rs, which should she pick?

Answer: C. ✔ This is the build-vs-buy sweet spot for a 240-person firm: the expensive engine is rented (like renting IaaS instead of pouring a data-center slab), the differentiating ingredient — Cypress's own data — stays theirs via RAG, the abstraction layer prices the exit (lock-in as a chosen gradient), and pilot-with-criteria is today's wave discipline in miniature. ✘ A is Refactor-priced thinking for a Replatform-sized problem: frontier-scale training runs on thousands of GPUs for weeks (Session 1), and "owning" a home-trained model buys a retailer risk, not safety. ✘ B is today's misconception incarnate — maximum spend, zero right-sizing, no success criteria, and the biggest model still can't answer from a catalog it has never been shown. ✘ D is Retain-everything as a reflex: a legitimate R for one system, but as a blanket strategy it simply gifts five years to competitors.

Q10 · The refund the robot approved 🧠 AI concept

Meadowlark Home Goods launched an AI customer-service assistant with a written acceptable-use policy and a company AI use log. In week three, the assistant confidently told a customer that a final-sale clearance item qualified for a full refund plus return shipping; the reply went straight to the customer, who screenshotted it. Meadowlark honored it — and 38 more like it before anyone noticed. Per today's governance minimum, which safeguard was missing?

Answer: B. ✔ Meadowlark had two of the four governance minimums (policy, log) and lacked the two that would have contained this: review tiers matched to stakes — an answer that commits company money is high-stakes by definition and needed a human gate before send — and an incident path, whose absence is why one bad refund quietly became thirty-nine identical ones. Governance doesn't promise the model never errs; it ensures errors meet a human before they meet money. ✘ A chases a model that doesn't exist — every model has an error rate, which is precisely WHY tiered review exists. ✘ C attacks the evidence, not the error: the promise was binding and wrong whether or not it was captured. ✘ D overcorrects a routing failure into abstinence — the assistant handled low-stakes questions fine; the flaw was which answers required sign-off, not whether AI may exist.

R1 · Sessions 1 — the evening rush

Anh's yoga-class app serves 800 users most of the day, but every evening from 6–8 PM demand triples as classes book up. Her cloud platform adds capacity automatically each evening, removes it by 9 PM, and her bill shows charges only for the extra instance-hours actually used. Which PAIR of cloud characteristics is Anh directly experiencing?

Answer: C. ✔ Capacity that stretches and shrinks with demand — automatically, in both directions — is rapid elasticity; billing that tracks the exact instance-hours consumed is measured service. Together they're why Anh never buys hardware for a 2-hour daily peak. ✘ A: both are true of almost any cloud app (reachable over networks, sharing pooled hardware) but neither describes the evening scaling behavior in the story. ✘ B: self-service is about provisioning without human intermediaries — nothing here required Anh to act at all; multi-tenancy is sharing, not scaling. ✘ D: virtualization is the enabling technology underneath, and grid computing splits one big job across machines — neither is the customer-visible characteristic being described.

R2 · Session 2 — the overnight upgrade

Verdant CRM runs one codebase serving 9,000 customer companies. Tuesday night Verdant ships a new reporting feature; Wednesday morning every customer has it, no installs anywhere. A confused customer asks their account manager: "When did your people install the upgrade on OUR server?" What's the accurate answer?

Answer: A. ✔ Multi-tenancy is THE SaaS economic engine from Session 2: one application instance, one upgrade, 9,000 beneficiaries — with tenant data logically isolated (the apartment-building model: shared building, locked units). It's why SaaS vendors ship features weekly while on-prem software shipped yearly. ✘ B describes the old managed-hosting world — 9,000 overnight manual updates is exactly the cost multi-tenancy eliminated. ✘ C is fiction with a security-incident flavor; SaaS requires no customer-side installation at all. ✘ D describes single-tenant deployment, which reintroduces per-customer upgrade lag — the opposite of "everyone woke up upgraded simultaneously."

R3 · Session 3 — the Tuesday casualty

Tomás runs Reef Supply's store on three identical rented virtual machines. During Tuesday's promotion, VM #2 hardware-fails outright — yet no customer notices anything: requests keep flowing to VMs 1 and 3 until a replacement boots. Which component made the failure invisible, and which service model is Tomás consuming?

Answer: B. ✔ Session 3's pairing: the load balancer health-checks its pool, detects VM #2's death, and silently routes around it — fault tolerance as a side effect of scale-out design. Renting raw VMs where you manage the OS and app is Infrastructure as a Service. ✘ A: firewalls filter traffic for security; they don't distribute load or mask dead servers — and rented VMs aren't SaaS (nobody hands Tomás finished software). ✘ C: snapshots restore state later; they do nothing in the seconds a machine dies, and managing your own VMs is below PaaS's abstraction line. ✘ D: a CDN caches static content at the edge — it can hide some read traffic but doesn't reroute app requests among your VMs; DBaaS is unrelated entirely.

R4 · Session 4 — day one, day last

At Osprey Financial, new analyst Dana had working access to all 14 business apps at 9:05 AM on day one — one login, one MFA prompt. Eight months later a contractor is terminated for cause, and by 9:06 AM every app, VPN, and mailbox is closed to them — one admin action. Which capability delivers BOTH experiences?

Answer: D. ✔ Session 4's core: when one identity provider vouches for users to every app (SSO/federation), provisioning becomes "create one identity, assign a role" and deprovisioning becomes one revocation that closes 14 doors at 9:06. The termination case is the security argument: orphaned accounts are a top breach vector. ✘ A: password strength doesn't create accounts or close them — a strong password on a forgotten account is a strongly-guarded unlocked door. ✘ B protects data on stolen hardware; irrelevant to app access lifecycle. ✘ C is the disease this cures: 14 separate account stores mean day-one takes weeks and termination misses at least one system — guaranteed.

R5 · Session 5 — seven cold years

Compliance requires Lagoon Grocers to keep 20 TB/year of store security-camera footage for seven years. Footage is written once, read essentially never (perhaps one clip pulled per quarter for an incident), and a retrieval delay of hours is acceptable. The current plan stores it all on the same premium block storage as the point-of-sale database, and the bill is climbing fast. What should the storage architect change?

Answer: B. ✔ Session 5's tiering logic: match the storage class to the access pattern. Write-once, read-almost-never, latency-tolerant data is the classic archive workload — object storage at archive tier costs a small fraction of premium block per GB, and a lifecycle rule automates the aging. The hot POS database keeps the expensive, low-latency block storage it genuinely needs. ✘ A pays sports-car prices to park in long-term storage — the exact waste pattern from Session 11's bill audit. ✘ C violates the seven-year compliance mandate; the fine dwarfs the savings. ✘ D reinvents 2003: unencrypted physical media, no durability, no auditability, and a per-store loss/theft surface — compliance officers have nightmares shaped like this option.

R6 · Session 6 — five time zones, zero decisions

Nimbus Design's ten-person team spans five time zones. Every decision waits for the weekly all-hands call (6 AM for two members, 11 PM for one), where half the hour is status recaps; documents are emailed as attachments, and "final_v7_REAL.docx" chaos reigns. Which change would most directly fix BOTH the decision latency and the version chaos?

Answer: C. ✔ Session 6's sync-vs-async framework: status and proposals are async-shaped work — a shared cloud doc gives one authoritative copy (killing v7_REAL forever via version history), comment threads that advance across time zones while others sleep, and an audit trail of the decision. Calls shrink to what needs real-time back-and-forth. ✘ A doubles the pain: more 6 AM meetings, more recap theater, same attachment chaos. ✘ B "solves" geography by discarding the talent-anywhere advantage that made the team possible — and is simply not a software architecture. ✘ D trades version chaos for zero record: verbal decisions across five time zones means five memories of what was decided, and no history at all.

R7 · Session 7 — the suspicious attachment

A client sends Meridian Forensics a suspicious executable to analyze. Analyst Petra wants to run it safely on her single workstation: fully isolated from her host OS and the network, with the ability to reset the environment to a pristine state in seconds after each detonation. Which Session 7 setup fits?

Answer: A. ✔ The Type 2 (hosted) hypervisor — VirtualBox-style, running atop her existing OS — is built for exactly this: a disposable guest OS whose isolation contains the malware, virtual networking switched off, and snapshots (Session 7's killer feature) rewinding to pristine in seconds. This is why security labs live in VMs. ✘ B is Russian roulette: logging out of email does nothing against ransomware encrypting the disk or malware installing persistence. ✘ C overshoots — physical isolation works but costs thousands, waits weeks, and can't "snapshot" hardware back to clean; the entire point of Session 7 is that virtualization made isolation cheap. ✘ D is folklore: renaming changes a label, not the payload, and one double-click undoes it.

R8 · Session 8 — the open bucket

Tidewater Legal stored client files in a cloud object-storage bucket. A journalist discovers the bucket was configured for public access — anyone with the URL could read case files. Tidewater's managing partner drafts an angry letter demanding the cloud provider accept liability, noting "we pay them to keep our data secure." Under the shared responsibility model, how does this actually resolve?

Answer: D. ✔ Session 8's dividing line decides this in the provider's favor: the storage service performed exactly as configured — the configuration was the customer's act. Providers secure facilities, hardware, and the service platform; customers own identity, access policy, and data classification. Public-bucket leaks are consistently customer-side misconfigurations (which is why providers now add warnings and "block public access" defaults). ✘ A: physical security never failed — no one broke into a data center; they clicked a public URL. ✘ B: the responsibility model exists precisely so accountability is assignable, not mystical. ✘ C: providers offer encryption tools, but deciding what's sensitive and who may access it cannot be outsourced — that judgment IS the customer's data governance duty.

R9 · Session 9 — fifteen minutes, not fifteen hours

Pelican Pay processes card transactions for 3,000 small merchants. The board sets targets: after any regional failure, service restored within 15 minutes (RTO) with essentially zero transaction loss (RPO ≈ 0). The CFO asks which DR architecture the targets force them to fund. What must the architect answer?

Answer: B. ✔ Session 9's ladder maps targets to tiers, and these targets sit at the top: RTO of minutes demands a second site already RUNNING (no build-out time exists inside 15 minutes), and RPO≈0 demands synchronous replication (every transaction lives in both regions before it's acknowledged). Active-active is expensive — and the targets, set by the board, are what justify it. ✘ A restores yesterday's data tomorrow: RTO measured in days, RPO in hours — three orders of magnitude off. ✘ C is honest mid-tier: hours of build-out violates the 15-minute RTO by design. ✘ D inverts the method: business impact sets targets, targets select architecture, architecture sets cost — "cheapest first" is how Session 1's flooded-closet stories begin.

R10 · Session 10 — the integration that kept breaking

BayBooks' distributor integration breaks roughly monthly: the distributor's team keeps changing internal field names and database structures, and each change ripples into BayBooks' order system, which was built to read the distributor's raw exports directly. Both companies are tired of the 2 AM fixes. What arrangement would end the monthly breakage?

Answer: A. ✔ Session 10's whole argument in one integration: a published contract makes the distributor's internals a black box — they can rename every column nightly, and as long as the API returns the promised JSON, BayBooks never notices (the loose-coupling animation's green flash). Versioning plus deprecation windows handles the day the contract itself must evolve. ✘ B is maximum tight coupling — every internal schema change now breaks BayBooks immediately, and a stray BayBooks query can degrade the distributor's production database. ✘ C embalms both businesses to avoid an architecture decision; the freeze breaks the first time either company must change. ✘ D adds a human to a machine problem: slower (daily), error-prone, and STILL breaks when the CSV's columns change — the coupling just moved into a spreadsheet.

R11 · Session 11 — green dashboard, red customers

CoveCommerce autoscales purely on average CPU: add servers above 70%, remove below 30%. During Monday's 9 AM login surge, customers wait 4+ seconds at checkout for ten painful minutes — yet average CPU peaked at 62%, so no scaling occurred and no alert fired (the only alert is "CPU > 85%"). The bottleneck turns out to be database connection queuing, which spikes latency long before CPU climbs. What should the ops team change?

Answer: C. ✔ Session 11's monitoring hierarchy: user-facing symptoms (latency, errors) are the truth; resource metrics (CPU, memory) are one possible cause among several. Here the pain lived in queuing while CPU stayed comfortable — so both the alert and the scaling trigger were watching the wrong window. Scaling and paging on p95 latency (plus queue depth) catches every future bottleneck whatever its cause. ✘ A tunes the wrong metric harder — 40% CPU alerts fire constantly during healthy load (hello, alert fatigue) and would STILL have missed a 62%-CPU incident. ✘ B blames automation for its configuration; the autoscaler followed its rule perfectly — the rule was wrong. ✘ D translates to "our architecture is the customer's problem," which is how competitors get their 9 AM traffic.

R12 · The synthesis — Coastal Bank returns

Coastal Bank & Trust (from your Session 1 quiz!) finished its modernization: the regulated core ledger stayed on bank-controlled private infrastructure; the marketing site and mobile-app backends run in public cloud, scaling out for campaign spikes and shrinking after; capacity beyond baseline bursts automatically; the board's quarterly report celebrates "IT spend now tracking usage instead of hardware cycles." Which combination of course concepts is Coastal Bank living, all at once?

Answer: D. ✔ Three threads of the whole course braided together: hybrid (Session 1) puts each workload where its requirements point — control for the regulated ledger, elasticity for spiky public workloads; scale-out + bursting (Sessions 1, 3, 11, 12) absorbs campaign spikes with temporary capacity that vanishes afterward; and "spend tracking usage instead of hardware cycles" is the CapEx→OpEx sentence from your very first quiz, now spoken by a board. ✘ A: the ledger explicitly stayed private (not pure public), spikes were handled by more instances (not bigger ones), and the bank celebrated reducing capital lock-in. ✘ B: no allied-organization sharing, no split-one-computation workload, and nobody celebrated tape. ✘ C: name-drops three real concepts, none of which appear in the story. P1 · Definition

A farmers' co-op debates two proposals: (a) rent a fixed physical server in a downtown data center on a 3-year lease, or (b) use a platform where they provision resources themselves in minutes, share pooled provider hardware, and get billed only for what they meter. A member asks: "Which one is actually cloud computing, and why?" The correct answer:

Answer: B. ✔ The defining characteristics — on-demand self-service, resource pooling, elasticity, measured service, network delivery — are all present in (b) and all absent in (a): a leased fixed server is just someone else's closet with a contract. ✘ A is the Session 1 misconception ("remote = cloud") that the five-characteristics test exists to kill. ✘ C inverts reality — the cloud's signature is no long-term commitment required. ✘ D is backwards: cloud computing is precisely computing without owning hardware. Compare this to pre-test P1: same concept, and you now have a five-point checklist instead of a vibe.

P2 · Service models

Four things crossed Rina's desk today. Which one is the SaaS?

Answer: D. ✔ Finished software, consumed through a browser, subscription-billed, zero customer-managed infrastructure — that's Software as a Service, end of test. ✘ A is IaaS: raw rented compute where you bring and manage the OS and everything above it. ✘ B is PaaS: a platform for developers' code — the customer brings applications, not just data. ✘ C isn't a cloud service model at all: colocation rents space, power, and cooling for hardware YOU own — the pre-cloud arrangement this course started by contrasting. (Pre-test P2 asked this with Gmail; if both felt easy, that objective is green.)

P3 · IaaS

An indie game studio rents 20 GPU-equipped virtual machines by the hour for a rendering push. They chose the machine sizes, installed their preferred OS and drivers, configured everything above the hypervisor, and will release it all next month. Which model are they consuming?

Answer: A. ✔ Rented compute, customer-selected sizing, customer-managed OS/drivers, released when done, billed by the hour: Infrastructure as a Service, straight from the definition — and note the elastic exit (releasing it next month) that owning could never offer. ✘ B stretches "software" past usefulness: SaaS means consuming a finished application; these VMs arrived empty. ✘ C misreads the word "platform" — PaaS abstracts the OS away so developers only bring code; this studio hand-installed drivers, which is life BELOW the PaaS line. ✘ D confuses control with location: they administer the OS, but the hardware is rented, pooled, and metered in a provider's building — the definition of not-on-premises.

P4 · Identity & SSO

At Bayfront Hospital, staff authenticate once each morning — one credential plus a code from their phone — and then move between the charting system, scheduling, email, and the pharmacy portal with no further logins. Behind the scenes, one system vouches for their identity to each application. What is this arrangement called?

Answer: C. ✔ One authentication event, one identity provider issuing trusted tokens to many applications — that's SSO, and the phone code is MFA strengthening the single front door (the door you fortify hardest because it now opens everything). ✘ A is an anti-pattern so notorious it appears in audit findings verbatim — shared credentials destroy accountability and survive employee departures. ✘ B protects stored data on disk; it has nothing to do with login flows. ✘ D is the subtle distractor (as it was on the pre-test): a password manager still performs a separate login to each app — four authentications, conveniently typed. SSO is ONE authentication, federated outward.

P5 · Virtualization

Corvid Analytics runs finance's regulated environment and HR's systems as fully isolated environments — separate OS instances, separate virtual disks and networks — on the SAME physical server, at the same time. When finance needs a risky upgrade, admins snapshot its environment first and roll back in minutes when the upgrade misbehaves. What technology makes all of this possible?

Answer: D. ✔ Every clue is a hypervisor feature: multiple isolated OS instances sharing one physical machine (the consolidation economics from Session 7), per-VM virtual disks and networks, and snapshots enabling minute-scale rollback. This is also Session 1's "engine of resource pooling" — the technology the whole cloud stands on. ✘ A changes clock speed; it creates zero isolation and adds instability. ✘ B spreads data across disks for speed/redundancy — a storage layer trick, not environment isolation. ✘ C controls network traffic BETWEEN systems; it cannot make one server host two operating systems, and it has no snapshot-rollback concept. (Pre-test P5 asked what virtualization lets one server do — today you named the machinery.)

P6 · Disaster recovery

After a ransomware tabletop exercise, Driftwood Outfitters' owner declares: "If we're hit, we MUST be selling again within two hours. And I can accept losing at most the last 15 minutes of orders — no more." Which pair of DR metrics did she just set, in order?

Answer: B. ✔ "Selling again within two hours" is Recovery Time Objective — the downtime clock; "lose at most 15 minutes of orders" is Recovery Point Objective — the data-loss window, which dictates backup/replication frequency (snapshots at least every 15 minutes, or continuous replication). These two numbers then SELECT the architecture tier and its price, as Session 9's slider lab demonstrated. ✘ A misuses SLA — that's a provider's contractual promise to you, not your internal recovery target. ✘ C swaps the definitions — the classic exam trap; anchor it as RTO=Time-to-restore, RPO=Point-of-last-good-data. ✘ D borrows hardware-reliability statistics (mean time between failures / to repair) that describe component behavior, not business recovery objectives.

P7 · SOA

Shorebird Tickets rebuilt its platform: catalog, cart, payment, and notification now run as independent programs, each owning its own data, each reachable only through a published JSON-over-HTTP interface, each deployable and scalable on its own. The mobile app and website are thin layers composing calls to these programs. What architecture is this?

Answer: A. ✔ Every SOA marker from Session 10 is present: decomposition by business capability, black-box services behind published contracts (JSON over HTTP), independent deployment and scaling, and front ends as thin orchestrators. This is the pre-test question you answered on definition alone — now you've built the /books API and broken the coupling demo yourself. ✘ B: ownership isn't architecture — a monolith is ONE program/deployment, and this is explicitly many, independently shipped. ✘ C: P2P distributes files among equal nodes with no service contracts or business capabilities. ✘ D: mainframe batch processes queued jobs on a schedule; nothing here batches, and everything here is request/response over a network.

P8 · Cloud economics

Pier 9 Provisions historically spent $90,000 every four years on server hardware (plus maintenance contracts), sized for a peak that occurred two weeks a year. After migrating, the CFO's report shows: no more four-year hardware purchases; instead, monthly bills that rise in the two peak weeks and fall the rest of the year. Which financial transformation is the report describing?

Answer: C. ✔ The $90K-every-four-years purchase was CapEx: cash locked into depreciating hardware sized for a two-week peak — meaning ~50 weeks of paying for idle capacity. The monthly usage-shaped bill is OpEx that breathes with the business. Note what the pre-test explanation warned and still holds: this shift wins on flexibility and risk, not automatically on totals — steady maxed-out workloads can still favor owning. ✘ A: nothing in the story concerns revenue; both columns are cost structure. ✘ B is the reverse direction — a trap that catches skimmers; read the arrows. ✘ D: restructuring WHERE money goes, transparently, on invoices, is accounting — the legal kind.
