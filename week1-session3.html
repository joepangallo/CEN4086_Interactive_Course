1 On-Demand Computing & the IaaS Contract

On-demand computing: capacity like tap water

This session opens with the idea that made IaaS thinkable: on-demand computing — getting computing capacity the moment you need it, in the amount you need, and releasing it the moment you don't. The lineage runs from 1960s time-sharing (many users renting slices of one mainframe) through "utility computing" (the dream of CPU-by-the-hour, like electricity) to 2006, when Amazon started renting virtual machines by the hour to anyone with a browser. Why it matters: demand for computing is lumpy — Harbor Lane needs 10× capacity on author-event nights and almost nothing at 4 AM — but owned hardware is a flat, fixed quantity you bought for the peak. On-demand computing lets capacity track demand, so money tracks value. Concrete example: a studio renting 100 machines for a weekend render, then deleting them Monday, having paid for exactly 48 hours. Misconception: "renting is always cheaper." Not always — a server running flat-out 24/7/365 can be cheaper to own, just as owning a car beats renting one daily. On-demand wins on bursts, uncertainty, and speed; the honest consultant models both (you did exactly this in Session 2's calculator).

IaaS defined — and the line you must memorize

Infrastructure as a Service rents you the fundamental computing resources — virtual machines (compute), storage volumes, and networking — while the provider owns and operates the physical world: data centers, power, cooling, hardware, and the virtualization layer that slices real machines into virtual ones. Everything above that line is yours: you choose and patch the operating system, install runtimes, deploy applications, and secure your data. It is the most flexible cloud model — you can run almost anything — and the most demanding, because "flexible" is a synonym for "your job." Examples of the product: Amazon EC2, Microsoft Azure Virtual Machines, Google Compute Engine. A virtual machine order sheet has five main knobs: vCPUs (processing cores), RAM, storage (attached disk, GB), region (which of the provider's data-center clusters worldwide — affecting price, latency to your users, and legal jurisdiction of the data), and the image (the OS snapshot it boots from). Misconception: "the cloud provider manages my server." In IaaS they manage the hardware under your server. If your OS goes unpatched, that breach is yours — a distinction with legal and quiz consequences.

2 Load Balancing & Monitoring — Keeping Rented Fleets Honest

Load balancing: the maître d' of your server fleet

Once you rent more than one server, you need a way to share the work. A load balancer is a network device (in the cloud, a rented service) that receives all incoming requests and distributes them across a pool of servers — round-robin (take turns) or least-connections (send work to the least busy) being the classic algorithms. Users see one address; behind it, any number of machines come and go. Why it matters twice: first, scale — it's the front door that makes Session 1's "scale out" strategy possible at all. Second, and less obvious: fault tolerance. The balancer health-checks every server continuously (an HTTP probe every few seconds); when a server hangs or dies, it's silently pulled from rotation and the survivors absorb its share — users never know. That same trick enables zero-downtime deploys: drain one server, upgrade it, return it to the pool. Concrete example: Harbor Lane's event-night crash was a single web server taking 100% of a 10× spike; three servers behind a balancer, with autoscaling adding more at 70% load, is the boring, correct fix. Misconception: a load balancer does not add capacity — it only spreads load across capacity you're paying for. Balancing three drowning servers gives you three evenly drowning servers.

Monitoring: you can't manage what you don't measure

This session pairs IaaS with system and network monitoring for a blunt reason: measured service means every rented hour is billed, so waste and trouble both show up as numbers — if you look. The starter kit: metrics (CPU %, memory, requests/min, latency, error rate — sampled continuously), dashboards (the graphs humans glance at), and alerts (rules that page a human or trigger automation when a threshold is crossed: "CPU > 80% for 5 minutes → add a server"; "monthly spend forecast > $1,500 → email the manager"). Alerts convert monitoring from wall art into a control system — and billing alarms are the difference between catching a forgotten test fleet in week one versus meeting it in the month-end invoice. Watch the per-server load bars below cross 70% and imagine the alert firing; in Session 11 you'll run a full incident-response dashboard built on these ideas.

3 Server Types, Cloud NAS & the Provider Market

Not all servers do the same job

"Server" is a role, not a shape — and today's catalog of server types matters because each role has a different resource appetite and a different scaling story. A web server terminates HTTPS and serves pages and images — light, stateless, and the easiest thing in computing to scale out (just add clones behind the balancer). An application server runs business logic — checkout rules, coupon math, tax calculation — CPU-hungrier, still usually clonable. A database server keeps the structured truth (orders, inventory) and answers queries — RAM- and disk-I/O-hungry, and the hardest to scale because all copies must agree; the classic pattern is one bigger primary plus read replicas. A cache server holds the hottest data in RAM (session tokens, the 500 bestselling covers) to answer repeat reads in ~1 ms and shield the database — RAM is nearly its whole diet. A file server holds shared documents at the OS-folder level. Why you care: sizing a VM (Segment 1) starts with asking which role it plays — a cache wants the RAM-heavy shape, a batch encoder wants CPU-heavy. Misconception: "one big server can just do everything." It can — right up until the roles fight over resources and you can't scale or troubleshoot any of them independently. Separating roles is step one of every real architecture, including your capstone diagram.

Cloud-based NAS: the file server, minus the closet

Network-Attached Storage (NAS) is shared file storage reached over a network — the "shared drive" every office knows. Cloud-based NAS delivers that same folders-and-files model as a rented service, so every location mounts the same authoritative storage instead of emailing copies around. What the provider adds beneath the familiar surface: automatic replication across hardware (a dying disk is their non-event, not your catastrophe), snapshots (yesterday's version of the folder, recoverable in clicks), capacity that grows by slider instead of by purchase order, and access control per user or store. Concrete example: Harbor Lane's 12 stores currently share files via a 9-year-old box with a clicking hard drive and no offsite copy — cloud NAS retires that single point of failure for tens of dollars a month. Misconception: NAS is not the same as consumer file-sync apps — NAS presents live shared folders to many machines; sync apps copy files to each device. (Block vs. object vs. file storage — the full taxonomy — is Session 5's opening act.)

The provider market — who actually sells IaaS

Objective 4 says "identify solution providers," so here is the field, provider-neutral: three hyperscalers dominate, dozens of smaller players (DigitalOcean, Linode/Akamai, Hetzner, OVH, Oracle Cloud) compete on price and simplicity. Browse the tabs — note how the same product (a VM) wears different names:

Amazon Web Services — the first mover (EC2 launched 2006) and largest provider. VMs are EC2 instances, in hundreds of sizes grouped by family: general purpose (m-series), compute-optimized (c-series), memory-optimized (r-series), GPU (p/g-series) — the families map directly to the server roles above. Block storage is EBS; the load balancer is ELB; 30+ regions worldwide. Strengths commonly cited: biggest service catalog and ecosystem. Watch-outs: a bill with hundreds of line items and real data-egress fees — monitoring (Segment 2) is not optional here.
Microsoft Azure — the enterprise and hybrid specialist. VMs are Azure Virtual Machines (D-series general purpose, E-series memory-optimized, N-series GPU). Strengths commonly cited: deep integration with Windows Server, Active Directory, and Microsoft 365 — organizations already on Microsoft identity often find Azure the path of least resistance — plus strong hybrid tooling for keeping some workloads on-premises (Session 1's hybrid model, productized). Its identity service (Entra ID) stars in Session 4. Watch-outs: licensing complexity is its own skill.
Google Cloud Platform — the engineering-culture pick. VMs are Compute Engine instances (e2 economy, n2 general purpose, a-series GPU). Historically notable for billing granularity (per-second billing arrived here first) and automatic sustained-use discounts — run a VM most of the month and the rate drops without paperwork. Strengths commonly cited: networking, data/analytics services, and Kubernetes (which Google originated). Watch-outs: smaller enterprise-support ecosystem than AWS/Azure in some industries.
Free tiers — real, but read the labels. Three different species: (1) Always-free allowances: Oracle Cloud's always-free ARM VMs and Google Cloud's e2-micro instance (select US regions) run indefinitely at $0 — small but genuinely useful for student projects. (2) Trial credits: $200–300 valid ~30–90 days (Azure, GCP) — powerful, then gone. (3) 12-month tiers: AWS's 750 hrs/month of small instances for a year. ⚠️ Most require a credit card for identity verification even when nothing is charged — this course never requires that; today's Lab C uses the providers' public pricing calculators, which need no account at all. Terms change often: verify on the provider's own free-tier page before believing any summary — including this one and especially an AI chatbot's.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · GPUs & AI Infrastructure — the hottest real estate in IaaS

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, while mastering Objective 4, you learn what AI actually runs on — why GPUs and not CPUs, why memory (not speed) is the wall, and why the answer to "should we just buy one?" is almost always no.

Modern AI is the loudest possible advertisement for IaaS, because it splits into two workloads with opposite shapes — and both are rented. Training a large model is a months-long marathon on thousands of GPU instances wired together with data-center-grade networking; frontier training runs are estimated in the tens or hundreds of millions of dollars of compute. It is the burstiest purchase imaginable: titanic capacity, needed intensely, then not needed at all — exactly the demand shape on-demand computing was invented for. Nobody rational buys 10,000 GPUs for a project that ends; even the biggest AI labs largely rent capacity from (or co-build with) cloud providers. Inference — serving your chat requests — is the opposite: millions of small, latency-sensitive requests, 24/7, spread across fleets of smaller GPU instances behind load balancers with autoscaling, precisely the Segment 2 architecture with pricier chips. Sizing vocabulary shifts, too: for AI instances, the headline spec isn't vCPUs but GPU class and VRAM — the model must physically fit in GPU memory, so VRAM plays the role RAM played in your VM Builder. When a provider lists a GPU instance at 30–100× the price of a web server, you're reading the economics of the entire AI industry in one pricing row.

The concept: wide beats fast — and memory beats both

Why GPUs? A CPU is a few brilliant generalists: a handful of powerful cores built to race through varied, branching work one task at a time — parse a request, check a password, update a row. A GPU is thousands of simple cores that all perform the same arithmetic on different numbers at the same instant. Neural networks are almost nothing but matrix multiplication — millions of independent multiply-adds that never wait on each other — exactly the shape GPUs were invented for (originally, coloring a million pixels at once). The GPU wins not because each core is faster — individually they're slower — but because it is thousands of lanes wide. Feed it AI math and it finishes in hours what a CPU grinds at for weeks.

The spec that decides whether a model runs at all isn't speed — it's VRAM, the GPU's on-board memory. Generating even one token reads essentially every parameter, so the whole model must sit in GPU memory: a 70-billion-parameter model at two bytes per parameter is roughly 140 GB of weights. No gaming card (16–24 GB) holds that, at any clock speed — you need data-center GPUs (80 GB class), usually several of them linked by fast interconnect. Misconception to bust: "any fast computer can run any AI model." Capacity decides if it runs; speed only decides how fast. VRAM plays the role RAM played in your VM Builder: check fit first, speed second.

Now the price tag. GPU instances rent for 10–100× a comparable CPU web server because the chips cost tens of thousands of dollars each, draw enormous power, need exotic cooling and networking, and are scarce. So why does nearly everyone rent them anyway? Demand shape — this session's favorite argument. A training cluster (thousands of GPUs, running for weeks, then nothing) is the burstiest purchase in computing; owned, it would idle most of the year while depreciating and being obsoleted by next year's chips. An inference fleet must autoscale with user traffic behind load balancers — Segment 2's architecture with pricier silicon. AI infrastructure is Session 1's rent-vs-own logic at maximum stakes, and the reason "GPU capacity" now headlines cloud-provider earnings calls.

Q1 · Whose patch was it anyway?

Marisol, IT lead at Pelican Point Outfitters, proudly migrates the web store from the office closet to two rented IaaS virtual machines. Four months later, attackers compromise both VMs through a Linux kernel vulnerability that had been patched upstream nine weeks earlier. The provider's incident report is blunt: physical hosts, network, and hypervisor were fully patched and uncompromised; the guest operating systems on Marisol's VMs had never been updated. Whose job was that patching, and why?

Answer: C. ✔ This is THE IaaS dividing line: the provider owns data centers, hardware, network, and hypervisor; the customer owns the guest OS, runtimes, applications, and data. Renting infrastructure rents the machine, not the administration of what you install on it — Marisol's team skipped nine weeks of updates on systems that were theirs to maintain. ✘ A describes a different, higher-abstraction purchase (managed hosting, PaaS, SaaS); if she wanted OS care included, IaaS was the wrong product. ✘ B — some providers offer optional auto-patching services, but they must be chosen and configured; nothing is automatic "by definition." ✘ D — upstream maintainers shipped the fix nine weeks early; applying fixes to running systems is the operator's job, and the operator here is Marisol. Session 8 formalizes this as the shared-responsibility model.

Q2 · Forty-eight hours of fur

Studio Marmoset must render a four-minute animated short — 6,000 frames at roughly 40 core-minutes each, about 4,000 core-hours — before Monday's festival submission deadline. The studio's eight workstations would need three weeks. There's no cash to buy servers, and after Monday the rendering need disappears entirely. What is the classic IaaS move?

Answer: A. ✔ A huge, parallelizable, hard-deadline, temporary workload is on-demand computing's founding use case: ~100 VMs working the weekend deliver ~4,000 core-hours, then the fleet — and the bill — evaporates. CapEx becomes a small, precise OpEx purchase shaped exactly like the need. (Note the echo of Session 1's grid-computing protein problem, now with rented iron.) ✘ B — procurement lead times alone (ordering, shipping, racking) blow the deadline, and reselling week-old depreciated hardware locks in a loss; every step fights the calendar. ✘ C is a category error: a SaaS office suite delivers finished productivity software; it cannot execute render jobs no matter how much storage it has. ✘ D — the festival deadline is fixed, and even "negotiable" deadlines spend reputation the studio needs. Renting beats both buying and surrendering.

Q3 · The Tuesday 2 PM mystery

BrightCart's web tier is four identical IaaS servers behind a load balancer. On Tuesday at 2 PM, server #3's application hangs — the process stays alive but stops answering requests. Support tickets that afternoon: zero. Revenue: normal. On Wednesday an engineer discovers #3 was effectively dead for six hours while no customer noticed anything. What most likely protected BrightCart?

Answer: D. ✔ A load balancer probes every backend continuously (e.g., an HTTP request to /healthz every few seconds). When #3 stopped passing probes, it was pulled from rotation automatically; the other three absorbed its quarter of the traffic (each rising from ~25% to ~33% of load — visible headroom permitting, no user impact). Fault tolerance arrives as a side effect of scaling out. ✘ A — shared storage, besides being an unusual web-tier design, cannot redirect HTTP requests away from a hung process; routing is the balancer's job. ✘ B contradicts the stem: Tuesday revenue was normal, meaning traffic flowed — it just avoided #3. ✘ C — DNS maps the site's name to the balancer's address; it neither detects nor works around a dead backend behind that address, and browsers can't serve dynamic checkout pages from cache. This is Segment 2's health-check story, verbatim.

Q4 · Milliseconds matter

GameDen's product pages assemble from database queries on every single view. Detail pages now take 340 ms, and the database server runs at 85% CPU — mostly re-answering identical queries about the same 200 bestselling games thousands of times per hour. Architect Ines says the fix is not a bigger database but one additional server TYPE. Which server, doing what?

Answer: B. ✔ Frequently read, rarely changed, wildly repetitive — that workload is the cache server's exact job description (the Redis/Memcached role). Hot results for the top 200 games live in RAM; repeat reads never touch the database; DB CPU collapses, page times fall from 340 ms toward tens of milliseconds, and the expensive database upgrade is deferred, maybe forever. ✘ A — file servers share documents between humans and systems; they don't accelerate query workloads, and images aren't the bottleneck here — repeated queries are. ✘ C — a balancer distributes work; it cannot answer queries or reduce the total repeated work reaching the database, and nothing says the existing balancer is saturated. ✘ D — backups protect against loss, serve zero production reads, and change latency not at all. Matching duty to server type is today's matcher game with money attached.

Q5 · The $4,100 surprise

In March, a developer at Opal Threads spun up six large test VMs for a one-day load test — and forgot them. Finance discovers the damage in MAY's statement: $4,100 for machines that idled at 2% CPU for nine weeks. The CEO's question to IT is precise: "What practice would have caught this in week one instead of week nine?" What should IT implement?

Answer: A. ✔ Measured service bills every provisioned hour, so waste is visible — but only to those who look. The professional kit: metrics collection (CPU, network, per-resource spend), dashboards for humans, and above all alert rules — "flag any VM under 5% CPU for 7 days," "email when the monthly forecast passes $1,500." A billing alarm converts this $4,100 lesson into a ~$450 one caught in week one. ✘ B — access control matters, but these VMs weren't breached; they were forgotten, and passwords don't page anyone about idleness. ✘ C — bandwidth changes how fast you reach servers, not whether anyone notices they exist. ✘ D — region choice sets the price multiplier (your VM Builder proves it); moving waste somewhere pricier just makes the surprise bigger. Session 11 builds an entire ops dashboard around this exact failure mode.

Q6 · Twelve stores, one spreadsheet, forty versions

Harbor Lane Books' twelve stores share inventory spreadsheets and HR documents by emailing copies to each other — nobody is sure which "FINAL_v7" is real. The master file server is a nine-year-old machine in the closet with a clicking hard drive and no offsite copy. Ops wants every store working from the SAME files, with automatic redundancy, without buying any hardware. Which IaaS-family service fits?

Answer: C. ✔ Cloud NAS delivers the familiar shared-folder model as a service: one authoritative copy visible to all twelve stores, replication across the provider's hardware (the clicking drive's death becomes a non-event), snapshots for "we deleted the payroll folder" moments, and capacity that grows by slider, not purchase order. It retires both the single point of failure and the FINAL_v7 chaos. ✘ A — GPU instances accelerate parallel computation (rendering, ML); they store nothing shared and are the priciest possible non-answer. ✘ B — bigger attachments industrialize the disease: more diverging copies, faster, still with no authoritative version. ✘ D — load balancers spread request traffic across servers; the stores have a storage-truth problem, not a traffic-distribution problem. Storage taxonomy goes deeper (block vs. object vs. file) in Session 5.

Q7 · Zero dollars, zero card

Professor Okafor's networking students must each keep a small Linux VM running for a ten-week semester project. Hard rules: genuinely $0, no credit card, running continuously. One student suggests signing up his dad's card for a premium trial "because it's refundable." Based on today's provider survey, what should the class actually use?

Answer: B. ✔ Today's free-tier taxonomy has three species: (1) always-free allowances — small resources, indefinitely (Oracle's ARM VMs, GCP's e2-micro in select US regions); (2) trial credits — $200–300 that expire in weeks; (3) 12-month tiers — like AWS's 750 hrs/month of small instances for a year, typically card-verified. A ten-week, always-on, tiny-VM project maps precisely onto species 1 — and terms change, so verify on the provider's own page first. ✘ A — false: always-free tiers are limited, conditional, and entirely real. ✘ C is fabricated — GPU instances are among the most expensive SKUs in any catalog and are never free on a schedule. ✘ D — forum "free clouds" are malware with a landing page, and dad's "refundable" card violates both the assignment's rules and common sense.

Q8 · The 3% server

Auditing Corvid Analytics' cloud account, Jae finds the marketing site's VM: 32 vCPU, 128 GB RAM, 200 GB storage in US East, provisioned "to be safe." Monitoring shows 3% average CPU and a 5 GB RAM peak. Under today's pricing model (vCPU $18, RAM $4.50/GB, storage $0.08/GB, US East ×1.00) it costs about $1,168/month. Jae proposes 4 vCPU / 16 GB / 200 GB — about $160/month. What is Jae doing, and what is the approximate annual saving?

Answer: D. ✔ Check the arithmetic: big VM = 32×18 + 128×4.50 + 200×0.08 = 576 + 576 + 16 = $1,168; right-size = 72 + 72 + 16 = $160; difference $1,008/month ≈ $12,096/year. Matching provisioned capacity to measured demand is called rightsizing — and at 3% CPU, even the smaller box has lots of room. "To be safe" oversizing is a pre-cloud reflex: when resizing takes weeks, you overbuy; when it takes minutes (elasticity!), you size to reality. ✘ A — scaling out means adding instances; Jae is shrinking one, and the figure is off by 12×. ✘ B — elasticity is capacity tracking demand automatically; a fixed oversized VM is its absence, and $500 is wrong. ✘ C names the wrong technique with the right number — nothing here distributes traffic; the fix is sizing, not balancing. Distractors with correct math and wrong concepts are how audits get argued, so know both.

Q9 · The fastest computer money can buy 🧠 AI concept

Aria's CEO returns from an electronics megastore with the most expensive gaming PC on the shelf — flagship CPU, record clock speeds, and a top gaming GPU with 24 GB of VRAM. "Fastest machine they make. Load up that open-source 70-billion-parameter model our competitors use." At full precision, the model's weights come to roughly 140 GB. Why is Aria about to disappoint the CEO?

Answer: D. ✔ Inference touches virtually all parameters for every token, so the whole model must live in VRAM; 140 GB into 24 GB doesn't go, at any clock speed. This is why data-center GPUs ship with 80 GB of memory and get ganged together over fast interconnect — and why they rent at prices no gaming PC hints at. (Running a smaller model, or a heavily compressed "quantized" version, is the realistic workaround — a different, humbler purchase.) ✘ A — open weights are free to download and run; the obstacle is physics, not law. ✘ B — overclocking multiplies operations per second, not gigabytes of memory; a faster engine doesn't enlarge the fuel tank. ✘ C — once weights are downloaded, local inference needs no internet at all. The gaming PC is a Ferrari being asked to haul a shipping container: impressive speed, wrong dimension.

Q10 · Own the mountain, or rent it? 🧠 AI concept

Ostara Labs fine-tunes its industry model twice a year — each run needs a 512-GPU training cluster for about three weeks. The rest of the year, its chatbot serves customers from a small GPU inference fleet that autoscales between 2 and 10 instances with daily traffic. A board member, horrified by the cloud invoices, proposes buying the 512 GPUs outright (~$15M, plus power, cooling, and replacement every few years): "We'd own our future." As CTO, what is the financially sound reply?

Answer: B. ✔ Do the utilization math: two 3-week bursts ≈ 6 weeks of use and ~46 weeks of idleness — an owned cluster sits ~88% wasted while it depreciates and next-generation chips obsolete it. Renting shapes spend exactly like demand: pay titanically for six weeks, near zero after — on-demand computing's founding case at AI scale. The inference side is Segment 2 economics with pricier silicon: autoscaling 2→10 with traffic only works when capacity rents by the hour. ✘ A — "owning always wins" fails at 12% utilization; ownership pencils out only for near-saturated, 24/7 workloads. ✘ C inverts the logic: the near-idle training cluster is the worst candidate to own; steadier inference would be the less-bad one, and even it is spiky. ✘ D — CPUs lack the parallel width for matrix math; the job would take months and cost more in total compute. Today's GPU-vs-CPU lesson, with an invoice attached.
