📝 Homework 1 · Lock the Front Door

Milestone 1 put your app on the internet. This homework is about the question every real operator asks the morning after launch: who, exactly, can walk in? You'll trace a real single sign-on login, dissect the token that makes it work, turn on real multi-factor authentication on an account you actually care about, and plan what happens the day an employee quits.

IndividualAll three project tracksDue at the start of Class 5Submit your GitHub repo link

Why this homework exists

Whatever track you're on, your Milestone 1 deployment has something in common with every other student's: it has no identity story at all. Anyone with the URL can reach everything it serves. Before Milestone 2 asks you to appraise identity solutions like a professional, this homework makes sure you've personally seen the four building blocks: federation (trusting someone else to say who a user is), tokens (the signed hall-pass that trust travels in), MFA (proving it's really you), and provisioning (granting and — more importantly — revoking access as people come and go).

What you'll need — all free, nothing to install

  • Your project repo and JOURNAL.md from Milestone 1
  • A web browser and your GitHub account
  • Your phone — with the GitHub Mobile app installed, or able to receive SMS text messages
  • The Inside the SSO Token interactive from this course

Part 1 · Trace a real SSO login

Pick any website or app where you sign in with a "Sign in with Google" / "Sign in with GitHub" / "Sign in with Apple" button — Spotify, Canva, Duolingo, a game, anything you genuinely use. You're going to log in once, slowly, and write down what actually happens.

  1. Do the login and watch the address bar. Note every domain the browser visits between clicking the button and landing back inside the app. There will be at least two different organizations' domains in that journey — that's the federation happening in front of you.
  2. Write the flow as a numbered play-by-play in hw1/answers.txt (.pdf or .docx also fine — just not .md) — one line per step, in order, from clicking the button to landing back inside the app signed in (redirect out → authenticate → token minted → redirect back → token verified → session begins). For each step, name who is acting: your browser, the app you were signing into, or the login provider.
  3. Label three things explicitly in your write-up: the identity provider (who checked your password), the service provider (who trusted the answer), and the trust relationship (the agreement between the two organizations that existed before you ever showed up).
  4. Explain it in your own words: why does the service provider believe the identity provider? What would the service provider have to build, store, and protect if that button didn't exist?
💡 Paste the actual domains you saw in the address bar next to each step — real evidence beats a textbook answer here.
Hand in: the numbered login trace with the three labels, plus your explanation, in hw1/answers.txt.

Part 2 · Inside the token

In Part 1, "token minted" was one arrow. This part zooms into that arrow. Work through the Inside the SSO Token interactive — anatomy, tamper lab, lifetimes, and kill switch — then answer the following in hw1/answers.txt:

  1. The three roles. For the login you traced in Part 1: who mints the token, who carries it, and who verifies it? One sentence each.
  2. The tamper question. In the tamper lab, you changed a claim inside the token and verification failed — even though you never touched the signature. Explain exactly why, in plain English, as if to a classmate who missed the session.
  3. The lifetime trade-off. A token that lives 30 days is convenient; a token that lives 5 minutes is safe. What specifically gets worse at each extreme?
🌊 Design task — Pelican Tours. Marissa is planning an admin panel for her nine tour guides. Guides log in from their phones, on boats, with spotty cellular signal. On Saturday at 2 PM, a guide's phone is stolen from a dock — logged in, session live. Write a short token policy for the guide app: how long an access token lives, whether/how it silently renews, and the exact kill-switch sequence — what the office does, which system does it, and how long the thief realistically keeps access under your policy. Justify each number with the boat-and-bad-signal reality; a policy that logs guides out mid-tour every five minutes will simply get disabled.
Hand in: answers to the three questions + the token policy, all in hw1/answers.txt.

Part 3 · Turn on real MFA — on an account that matters

Your GitHub account now guards your project repo, your journal, and every deliverable in this course. Time to protect it like it matters, because it does.

  1. On GitHub: click your avatar → Settings → Password and authentication. Use GitHub Mobile or SMS/Text message as your method — either is fine for this course.
  2. If you see an Enable two-factor authentication button, click it and choose SMS/Text message as the setup method (the wizard suggests an authenticator app first — pick SMS instead). Enter your phone number and confirm the code GitHub texts you.
  3. Prefer GitHub Mobile (push approval on your phone instead of texted codes)? Install the GitHub app and sign in — it appears under Two-factor methods automatically once 2FA is on, and you can set it as your Preferred 2FA method.
  4. If your account already says Enabled — GitHub requires 2FA for many accounts — set your Preferred 2FA method to GitHub Mobile or SMS/Text message and confirm that method works.
  5. Under Recovery options, GitHub shows recovery codes. Save them somewhere safe and private — a password manager or printed and put away.
  6. Log out, log back in, and experience the second factor doing its job.
⚠️ Recovery codes are secrets. Never screenshot them, never paste them in your journal, never commit them to a repo — public or private. Your screenshot below must show only the settings page saying two-factor is enabled, with no codes visible.

Then write two short paragraphs in hw1/answers.txt:

  1. Classify the factors. Your password, the login code texted to your phone, and a fingerprint are three different kinds of proof. Name the three factor categories and place each example in one.
  2. The AI attacker. Session 4's AI Spotlight covered deepfakes and voice cloning. Rank these four second factors from weakest to strongest against an attacker armed with today's AI tools — SMS codes · TOTP authenticator app · voice verification ("say your passphrase") · passkey/hardware key — and justify the top and bottom of your ranking. Hint: think about which factors an AI can imitate versus which it would have to physically possess.
Hand in: screenshots/hw1-mfa-enabled.png (no codes visible!) + the two paragraphs in hw1/answers.txt.

Part 4 · The guide who quits

🌊 It's Friday, 5 PM, peak season. One of Marissa's nine guides quits — on bad terms — effective immediately. That ex-guide's phone still has: a login to the future admin panel, the shared photo-archive password taped to the office wall, and membership in the "Guides" group chat where tomorrow's customer manifests get posted.

Every identity system lives or dies on three lifecycle moments — the joiner (day one: grant exactly what the role needs), the mover (role changes: adjust, don't accumulate), and the leaver (departure: revoke everything, fast, completely). You're going to plan the leaver twice, in two different worlds:

  • World A — passwords live in the app. Every tool (admin panel, photo archive, chat) has its own account per guide, or worse, shared passwords.
  • World B — one central identity. Every tool trusts a single identity provider; guides sign in to everything through it.

Build a table in hw1/answers.txt (plain-text columns are fine; a real table if you chose .docx or .pdf) with one row per access the ex-guide holds, and for each world: what must be done, who does it, in which system, and what happens if that row is forgotten. Then finish with one recommendation paragraph: which world should Marissa run at 9 guides — and does your answer change at 900 employees? Be honest about cost and effort, not just security.
