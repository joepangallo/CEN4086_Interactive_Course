1 What "Cloud" Really Means

From Web 2.0 to the Cloud

In the early web (call it Web 1.0), pages were read-only brochures: a company published, you read. Web 2.0 changed the direction of data — users began writing to the web: posting, uploading, commenting, collaborating. Think of the difference between reading a restaurant's homepage and posting a review with photos. That shift created a problem: if millions of users generate content and expect instant access from any device, who supplies all the servers, storage, and bandwidth? No single company could predict demand well enough to buy the right amount of hardware. The answer became the cloud: massive shared data centers whose resources are rented out on demand, over the internet, and billed like a utility.

The key insight is that the cloud is an abstraction. When you flip a light switch, you don't know (or care) which power plant made your electricity. When you save a photo "to the cloud," you don't know which physical disk in which building holds it — and that's the point. You interact with a simple interface (a URL, an app, an API) while a provider manages racks of real hardware, cooling, power, staff, and security behind the curtain. You trade control of hardware for freedom from hardware.

2 Deployment Models & Service Models

Four deployment models — where the cloud lives and who shares it

A public cloud (AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud) rents shared infrastructure to anyone with a credit card — maximum pooling, minimum cost, fastest start. A private cloud dedicates cloud-style infrastructure to a single organization, on-premises or hosted, trading cost for control — chosen when regulation or secrecy demands it. A community cloud is shared by organizations with common needs — five hospitals sharing a HIPAA-compliant platform split costs while meeting shared rules. A hybrid cloud combines them: keep sensitive systems private, burst everyday workloads to the public cloud. Most real enterprises today end up hybrid — remember that for the capstone.

Rule of thumb: IaaS = rent the computer (you install everything above the hypervisor). PaaS = rent the platform (you bring only code + data). SaaS = rent the finished software (you bring only your data and users). The syllabus devotes Sessions 2–3 to these — today you just need the shape.

Beyond the big three: Anything-as-a-Service (XaaS)

One more foundation before the labs: "anything as a service." The industry now offers nearly everything as a metered service — browse the tabs:

Identity as a Service. Outsourced login, single sign-on, and multi-factor authentication (Okta, Microsoft Entra ID). Instead of every app storing its own passwords, one identity service vouches for users everywhere. Entire Session 4 topic — and a capstone deliverable.
Storage as a Service. Durable, metered storage over the internet (Amazon S3, Google Cloud Storage, Dropbox). Pay per gigabyte-month; the provider worries about failed disks — they keep multiple copies automatically. Session 5 topic.
Database as a Service. A managed database (backups, patching, replication handled) that your app connects to — e.g., Amazon RDS, MongoDB Atlas free tier. You design tables; you never SSH into the database server.
Disaster Recovery as a Service. Your systems continuously replicate to a provider's cloud; when your building floods, you fail over and run from their copy. Session 9 shows why RTO/RPO targets decide what tier you buy.
Functions as a Service (serverless). Upload a single function; the provider runs it on demand, billing per invocation — no servers visible at all (AWS Lambda, Cloudflare Workers free tier). The logical endpoint of the abstraction story.
AI as a Service. The newest arrival: rent intelligence by the token. Chatbots (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude) are AI-SaaS; their APIs are AI-PaaS for developers; the GPU fleets underneath are IaaS. One product, all three models — today's AI Spotlight unpacks this.

3 Scalability, Virtualization & Grid Computing

Scalability: up vs. out

When traffic grows, you have two moves. Scale up (vertical): replace your server with a bigger one — simple, but there's a ceiling (the biggest machine money buys) and a single point of failure. Scale out (horizontal): add more copies of a smaller server behind a load balancer that spreads requests across them — nearly unlimited, and one dead server barely matters. The cloud's superpower is elasticity: scaling automatically, in both directions, so capacity tracks demand hour by hour. A pizza shop analogy: scale up = buy one giant oven; scale out = add more regular ovens and a person routing orders; elasticity = ovens appear at dinner rush and vanish at midnight, and you pay per oven-hour.

Virtualization & grid computing — two ideas that made the cloud possible

Virtualization (preview of Session 7): software called a hypervisor lets one physical server host many isolated virtual machines. Before virtualization, the average corporate server ran at 5–15% utilization — racks of expensive machines mostly idling. Virtualization lets providers pack many customers' VMs onto shared hardware safely. It is the engine behind resource pooling; without it, cloud economics collapse.

Grid computing: chop one enormous job into pieces and run the pieces on many machines at once. Famous example: SETI@home borrowed millions of idle home PCs to analyze radio-telescope data. The grid mindset — "many cheap machines beat one giant machine" — became the cloud's scale-out philosophy. But coordination isn't free: splitting work, shipping data, and merging results adds overhead, so doubling machines never quite halves the time.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · Training vs. Inference — and why the cloud is where AI lives

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, while mastering Objective 1, you learn what an AI model actually is — and why its two lives (training and inference) are both cloud stories.

This course weaves AI through every session for a simple reason: modern AI is the most extreme cloud workload ever created, and it exercises every concept in this course. Training a large language model consumes thousands of GPU servers for months — that's IaaS at maximum scale. Serving the model to millions of chat users requires load balancing, autoscaling, and global data centers — that's elasticity. Developers who call a model through an API are consuming a platform service, and the chatbot your aunt uses to write birthday poems is pure SaaS. And the "grid computing" idea you met in Segment 3 — splitting huge jobs across machines — is the direct ancestor of today's GPU training clusters.

The connection runs both ways — the cloud also increasingly runs on AI: providers use machine learning to predict demand, place workloads, detect attacks, and cool data centers. By Session 12 you'll have seen AI as a SaaS product, a PaaS building block, an IaaS consumer, an identity threat, a storage pattern (vector databases), a security tool, and a migration assistant.

The concept: a model is learned, then served

An AI model is not a database of stored answers — that's the single most common misconception. A model is millions to billions of numeric parameters whose values were learned from data. It has two completely different lives:

1 · Training — the model is built. Enormous datasets are pushed through the network again and again while an algorithm nudges the parameters toward better predictions. For a large language model this means thousands of GPUs running in parallel for weeks — a direct descendant of the grid computing you met in Segment 3, and the most extreme burst-IaaS workload on Earth. It happens rarely and costs a fortune up front.

2 · Inference — the finished model is used. Your prompt goes in, flows once through the learned parameters, and an answer comes out in seconds. Inference is small per request but happens billions of times a day, which is why it needs load balancing and elasticity — and it's billed by the token (a chunk of text, roughly ¾ of an English word). Pay-per-token is the purest example you'll ever see of the cloud's measured service trait applied to intelligence itself. One more consequence of generating (not retrieving): ask the same question twice and you'll usually get different wording — the model samples fresh each time. You'll prove this yourself in Lab B.

📝 Session Quiz — Story Problems

Ten workplace scenarios — the last two test today's AI concept. Pick the best answer; every question explains all four options after you answer. Counts toward the syllabus "Exercises/Quizzes" grade (30%).

Q1 · The flooded closet

Rosa owns a 14-person accounting firm in Tampa. Client files live on one server in a hallway closet; her nephew backs it up to a USB drive "most Fridays." A pipe bursts in July — server and drive are soaked, and it's the middle of tax-extension season. Which cloud characteristic would have MOST directly prevented Rosa's data loss?

Answer: C. ✔ Rosa's core failure is that all copies of the data shared one room. Cloud storage keeps redundant copies across devices — and, configured properly, across regions — inside professionally managed data centers, so one burst pipe destroys nothing. ✘ A (elasticity) addresses capacity swings, not data survival; her firm's load is steady. ✘ B (metering) is a billing property — it changes what she pays, not whether data survives. ✘ D (self-service) would help her provision quickly after the disaster, but by then the files are already gone; it prevents delay, not loss.

Q2 · The CFO's spreadsheet

Devon, CFO of a startup, must choose between buying $180,000 of servers (replaced every 4 years) or renting equivalent cloud capacity for about $4,200/month. The board asks: "Beyond the totals, what's the fundamental FINANCIAL difference?" What should Devon say?

Answer: B. ✔ This is the CapEx→OpEx shift: cash isn't locked into depreciating hardware sized for a guess about year-4 demand; spend tracks reality and can be turned off. ✘ A is false — do the math: $4,200 × 48 months = $201,600 > $180,000. Steady, predictable, fully utilized workloads can favor owning; the cloud wins on flexibility and avoided risk, not automatically on totals. ✘ C — someone still configures, secures, and monitors cloud systems; staff costs shift, not vanish. ✘ D ignores demand uncertainty, hardware refresh cycles, and the utilization problem (most owned servers idle).

Q3 · The hospital alliance

Five independent rural hospitals want to share one electronic-records platform. All face identical HIPAA obligations; none can afford a private build alone; public multi-tenant SaaS makes their compliance officers nervous about co-tenants. Which deployment model fits BEST?

Answer: A. ✔ This is the classic community-cloud case: allied organizations, shared compliance regime, shared cost — controls tailored to their common rules without each paying private-cloud prices. ✘ B ignores the stated compliance concern about arbitrary co-tenants (public cloud CAN be HIPAA-compliant, but the scenario says their officers require a narrower trust boundary). ✘ C quintuples cost and defeats the purpose of sharing — exactly what "none can afford alone" rules out. ✘ D trades manageable digital risk for fire, loss, illegibility, and zero disaster recovery — paper is not "safe," it's just differently fragile.

Q4 · Launch day at NoodleNow

Priya's food-delivery app normally serves 2,000 requests/min on 4 servers. A viral TikTok sends 40,000 requests/min for six hours; by midnight traffic is normal again. Her cloud platform automatically grew to 80 servers and shrank back, billing her for exactly the hours used. Which characteristic did Priya just experience?

Answer: D. ✔ Capacity stretched 20× automatically and contracted when demand fell (elasticity), and billing followed actual usage (measured service) — the pair that makes viral moments survivable without owning 80 idle servers year-round. ✘ A (network access from many devices) was present but isn't what saved her — it didn't change capacity. ✘ B is the underlying enabler (VMs spin up fast because they're virtual), but the characteristic Priya experienced is the scaling behavior, not the hypervisor mechanics. ✘ C — grid computing splits ONE huge computation across machines; Priya had millions of small independent requests, the opposite shape of workload.

Q5 · The dead zone

Marcus manages building inspectors who work in rural areas with no cell coverage. A vendor pitches moving ALL inspection software and forms to a cloud-only SaaS. Marcus hesitates. Which cloud DISADVANTAGE is he right to worry about, and what's the standard mitigation?

Answer: B. ✔ Cloud services presume a network path; inspectors in dead zones would be unable to work. The standard pattern is offline-first design: the app stores work locally and synchronizes when connectivity returns — a deliberate architecture choice Marcus should demand from the vendor. ✘ A is a real cloud risk but not THIS scenario's problem, and "never sign contracts" isn't a mitigation, it's avoidance. ✘ C reverses the economics — cloud reduces capital costs; buying servers wouldn't give inspectors connectivity in the field anyway. ✘ D — multi-tenancy is how public clouds work at all; a customer can't "ban" co-tenants, and co-tenants aren't why an app fails offline.

Q6 · Twelve sleepy servers

An audit of Bayfront Logistics finds 12 physical servers averaging 8% CPU utilization, each running one application "so they can't interfere with each other." The data center is out of rack space and the CIO wants to add three more apps. What should the infrastructure team propose FIRST?

Answer: C. ✔ 8% utilization is the classic pre-virtualization disease. A hypervisor preserves the isolation they wanted ("can't interfere") while packing many VMs per host — typical consolidation frees 70–85% of hardware, solving the rack-space problem with room for the three new apps. ✘ A spends money to make the waste bigger. ✘ B destroys business function to work around an infrastructure inefficiency — backwards. ✘ D — speed isn't the constraint (they're at 8%!); overclocking adds heat, risk, and zero rack space. This is Session 7's entire topic; you'll run this consolidation in a simulator.

Q7 · The protein puzzle

A university lab must evaluate 50 million candidate molecules against one protein model. Each evaluation is independent and takes ~2 seconds on a standard core. On the lab's single 64-core server the job needs about 18 days, but the conference deadline is in 3 days. Which approach fits the problem's SHAPE best?

Answer: A. ✔ Fifty million independent tasks is the perfectly parallel workload grid computing was born for: 600 cloud cores ≈ under 2 days, 1,200 cores ≈ under a day — rented only for the run. This is exactly how modern research (and AI training data prep) uses the cloud. ✘ B — even a server 6× faster (barely buildable) still needs ~3 days with zero margin, and it costs vastly more than renting burst capacity. ✘ C confuses a delivery model with a computation strategy — SaaS-ifying the tool doesn't make the math finish sooner. ✘ D — the bottleneck is compute time per evaluation, not file size; compression saves disk, not CPU-seconds.

Q8 · The cautious bank

Coastal Bank & Trust wants a modern public website with seasonal marketing campaigns (spiky traffic), but regulators and internal policy require the core-banking ledger to remain on infrastructure the bank exclusively controls. The CTO wants ONE coherent strategy, not two disconnected worlds. What should she choose?

Answer: D. ✔ Hybrid is purpose-built for this split: each workload goes where its requirements point — control for the regulated ledger, elasticity and low cost for the spiky marketing site — with secure networking making them one system. ✘ A bets the banking license on regulators "adapting"; compliance isn't optional. ✘ B forces the bank to buy peak capacity for marketing spikes that public cloud rents by the hour — maximum cost for the least sensitive workload. ✘ C — community clouds serve shared needs among allied orgs; a marketing site isn't a shared regulatory workload, and competitors don't co-own ledgers. Coastal Bank returns as a story problem in Sessions 8 and 12.

Q9 · The token-shaped bill 🧠 AI concept

Tanvi's startup added an AI chat helper to its website using a big provider's model. The first invoice arrives priced per million tokens processed. Her CFO is baffled: "Why can't we just buy the software once like we did with our accounting package?" Which explanation is correct?

Answer: B. ✔ Every reply consumes real GPU compute at request time (inference). The provider meters that work in tokens — text chunks of roughly ¾ of a word — exactly how the cloud meters storage in GB and VMs in hours. It's Objective 1's "measured service" applied to intelligence. ✘ A — a one-time license can't work economically: the seller pays compute costs every single time Tanvi's customers chat. ✘ C — "token" here is a text unit, not currency; unrelated to crypto. ✘ D — retraining is the rare, expensive training phase; answering questions never retrains the model. Confusing the two phases is the classic mistake this session warns against.

Q10 · The do-it-yourself LLM 🧠 AI concept

The CEO of Harborview Analytics announces: "Cloud AI fees are a waste. Buy us one top-of-the-line server and we'll train our own ChatGPT-class model in the office." As lead engineer, what's the accurate response?

Answer: C. ✔ Training is the heavyweight phase: it parallelizes one colossal job across thousands of GPUs for weeks — the direct descendant of today's grid computing segment, and precisely the burst workload the cloud exists to rent. A single machine is off by a factor of thousands. The pragmatic path for a small firm is consuming inference as a service, or renting a modest training fleet for a small custom model. ✘ A/B invert the phases — inference is the light per-request work; training is the mountain, and two servers instead of one dents nothing. ✘ D — no such general licensing law exists; the obstacle is compute scale and cost, not legality.
