📝 Week 1 Mastery Quiz

Eight story problems spanning all three sessions. Every question explains all options after you answer.

Q1 · The flooded closet (S1)

Rosa's firm keeps client files on one closet server, backed up to a USB drive in the same room. A pipe bursts; both are soaked. Which cloud characteristic would MOST directly have prevented the loss?

Answer: C. ✔ Every copy shared one room; cloud storage keeps redundant copies across devices and regions. ✘ A is about capacity swings. ✘ B is billing. ✘ D speeds provisioning after the fact — too late.

Q2 · The cautious bank (S1)

Coastal Bank wants a spiky public marketing site but must keep its core ledger on infrastructure it exclusively controls, as one coherent strategy. Best choice?

Answer: D. ✔ Each workload goes where its requirements point — control for the ledger, elasticity for the site. ✘ A ignores compliance. ✘ B overpays for marketing spikes. ✘ C is for shared regulatory needs among allied orgs, not a marketing site.

Q3 · The apartment worry (S2)

"Multi-tenant means my data is mixed with my competitor's." Accurate response?

Answer: B. ✔ Isolation is enforced on every query; the legitimate concerns are noisy neighbors and blast radius. ✘ A confuses shared infrastructure with shared data. ✘ C/D misunderstand SaaS.

Q4 · The renewal shock (S2)

A PaaS vendor raises prices 40%; leaving means rewriting every feature built on its proprietary APIs. What is this, and when is it prevented?

Answer: B. ✔ High switching cost = lock-in, mitigated while building, not while leaving. ✘ A/C/D are unrelated concepts.

Q5 · Whose patch was it? (S3)

A firm moves its store to two rented IaaS VMs. Months later both are breached through an unpatched guest-OS kernel bug; the provider's hosts and hypervisor were fully patched. Whose job was the guest-OS patching?

Answer: C. ✔ The IaaS dividing line: provider owns hardware + hypervisor; customer owns guest OS, runtimes, apps, data. ✘ A describes managed hosting/PaaS/SaaS. ✘ B — auto-patching must be chosen. ✘ D — applying fixes is the operator's job.

Q6 · The 3% server (S3)

An audit finds a marketing-site VM at 32 vCPU / 128 GB provisioned "to be safe," running at 3% CPU. Right-sizing it to 4 vCPU / 16 GB cuts the bill from ~$1,168 to ~$160/month. What is this, and the rough annual saving?

Answer: D. ✔ Matching capacity to measured demand is rightsizing: ($1,168−$160)×12 ≈ $12,096/yr. ✘ A adds instances (off by 12×). ✘ B misnames it. ✘ C names the wrong technique with the right number.

Q7 · The Tuesday 2 PM mystery (S3)

Four IaaS servers sit behind a load balancer. Server #3's app hangs for six hours; zero support tickets, normal revenue. What most likely protected the site?

Answer: D. ✔ Continuous health probes detect the hang and quietly reroute — fault tolerance as a side effect of scaling out. ✘ A can't reroute HTTP. ✘ B contradicts "normal revenue." ✘ C — DNS points at the balancer; it can't work around a dead backend.

Q8 · The fastest computer money can buy (🧠 AI)

A CEO buys the top gaming PC (24 GB VRAM) to run a 70-billion-parameter model whose weights are ~140 GB at full precision. Why won't it work?

Answer: D. ✔ Inference touches nearly all parameters per token, so the whole model must live in VRAM; 140 GB into 24 GB doesn't go at any clock speed. ✘ A — open weights are legal. ✘ B — overclocking adds speed, not memory. ✘ C — local inference needs no internet once weights are downloaded.
