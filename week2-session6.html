1 From Email to Co-Editing

Web-based collaboration begins with email

The story starts where collaboration actually started: email — the first killer app of the networked office. Email is asynchronous and store-and-forward: you send when convenient, they read when convenient, and the message waits patiently in between. For coordinating across time zones, that design is genuinely brilliant, which is why email refuses to die. But use email to co-produce a document and its greatest strength becomes the bug: every attachment is a fork. Send Budget.xlsx to five people and there are now six diverging copies of the truth; two of them edit simultaneously and someone gets the soul-crushing job of manually merging cell by cell. Add no presence (is anyone even working on this?), reply-all storms, and 25 MB attachment limits, and you have the pain that cloud collaboration was invented to end.

The cloud's move: one master copy

Cloud document collaboration (Google Docs/Sheets/Slides, Microsoft 365 web) inverts the model: instead of copies traveling to people, people travel to the single master copy, which lives in the cloud. Everyone edits THE document; the service merges keystroke-level changes in real time, shows colleagues' cursors (presence), tracks version history (a time machine — restore any earlier state), and layers on comments, @mentions, and suggesting mode so review happens in the artifact instead of around it. The same one-master-copy idea powers shared calendars (free/busy state instead of "when are you free?" email chains) and shared task boards. Why it matters for Objective 6: "differentiate collaboration solutions" starts with recognizing which model a tool uses — traveling copies (email attachments) or a shared master (cloud docs) — because everything else (version chaos or version history) follows from that choice.

Common misconception: "real collaboration means everyone together, live." Often the opposite: across time zones and deep-work schedules, async collaboration — comments, suggesting mode, recorded video, written updates — produces better-thought-out input than forcing twelve people into one meeting. 2 Picking the Stack: Criteria & the Completeness Meter

Tool selection criteria — the checklist behind the demo gloss

Every collaboration vendor's demo looks wonderful. The practical point is that selection is an evaluation exercise, and the criteria are mostly unglamorous: Cost — free tiers are real but have real edges (Zoom's free meetings cap at 40 minutes; Slack's free plan hides messages after 90 days). Integration & SSO — Session 4 callback: can every tool federate with ONE identity provider? If a tool can't join your SSO, every hire and every termination now has a manual exception… you know from the lifecycle simulator exactly how that movie ends. Security & compliance — where is data stored, who can export it, does it meet your retention rules (Session 5 callback)? Adoption & training — the best tool nobody opens loses to the decent tool everyone already knows. Data export — vendor lock-in (Session 2 callback) applies to your chat history too: can you take it with you? Vendor viability — will this company exist in five years?

Common misconception: "more tools = more collaboration." Each added tool is another place where truth can hide, another notification stream, another login (and another SSO integration to maintain). Consolidated suites (Google Workspace, Microsoft 365) win on integration; best-of-breed stacks win on individual features and lose on seams. There is no free lunch — only a deliberate trade.

3 Meetings, Streaming & the Price of Sync Time

Web meetings and scheduling across organizations

Cloud meeting tools (Meet, Zoom, Teams) beat the old conference call because they carry more than voice: screen sharing turns "go to slide 12" into showing slide 12; recording makes the meeting an artifact absent people can watch; live captions make it accessible and searchable. Cloud scheduling solved a quieter problem: calendars became shared state, so free/busy lookup replaces the seven-email "does Thursday work?" dance — and booking pages (Calendly-style, Cal.com free) extend that across organizational borders, which is why your dentist now sends you a link instead of playing phone tag.

Streaming video as a collaboration tool

Streaming video/TV grew up in entertainment and matured into three workplace forms: live streams (town halls, launch events — one-to-many, with chat Q&A), webinars (structured presentations to outsiders), and — most useful per minute — the on-demand library: recorded trainings and demos employees watch at need. For Harbor Lane, a 12-store chain, that's the difference between flying a trainer to twelve stores and recording one excellent 15-minute POS training every new hire watches on day one. Live = shared moment and immediate questions; recorded = perfect repetition at zero marginal cost. Different tools, deliberately chosen — again.

🧠 AI Concept of the Day · Hallucination — why fluent ≠ true, and how teams verify AI work

AI concepts track: every session teaches one real AI concept through that day's cloud objective. Today, while completing Objective 6's collaboration half (and prepping for the midterm), you learn why language models sometimes state falsehoods with total confidence — and the verification workflow that makes AI a safe collaborator anyway.

Collaboration tools were the first workplace software to get a visible AI upgrade, because meetings and documents are exactly what language models are good at chewing on. AI meeting assistants (built into Meet, Teams, Zoom, and add-ons) now produce live transcription with speaker attribution, a summary, and an extracted action-item list minutes after the call — which quietly changes the sync/async math from Segment 1: if a meeting auto-produces a readable artifact, people who skip it lose less, so fewer people need to attend live. AI in documents drafts, rewrites, translates, and tightens — a first-draft partner living inside the co-editing surface. Two cautions before you trust any of it: models still hallucinate (a summary can state a decision the meeting never made — and an unchallenged wrong summary becomes the official record), and consent matters: recording plus AI processing of colleagues' words is a policy decision, announced at the start of the call, not a default.

The concept: a language model does not look facts up — it generates the most plausible continuation of the text in front of it, one token at a time, from patterns learned in training (Session 1). Most of the time the most plausible continuation happens to be true. But when grounding is thin — an obscure topic, a garbled transcript, a question about a document it never saw — the machinery doesn't stop; it keeps producing something shaped like an answer. That is a hallucination (researchers prefer confabulation): fluent, grammatical, specific, and wrong. Invented citations arrive with page numbers; invented people come with plausible job titles; invented statistics carry decimal points. This kills the intuition you bring from human colleagues — that confidence and specificity signal knowledge. In a text generator, a confident tone and precise detail are style properties, nearly free to produce, and exactly as easy to generate for a false statement as for a true one.

Why meeting assistants raise the stakes — and the workflow that lowers them: an AI meeting assistant is a pipeline: a speech-to-text model produces the transcript, then a summarization pass compresses it. Errors compound across stages — a mumbled "fifteen percent" transcribed as "fifty percent" reaches the summarizer as clean text, gets restated as a confident decision, and the wrong number is now in the official minutes with nobody's name on the mistake. Teams that use AI well wrap it in a verification workflow: (1) source-of-truth check — verify claims against the recording, the document, the invoice, never against the AI's own restatement; (2) "show your evidence" prompts — ask where in the transcript a decision was made, and treat "no quote available" as your answer; (3) sign-off tiers by stakes — a brainstorm needs no review, anything that becomes a record, an obligation, or someone's to-do gets a human check, and client-facing numbers get sign-off, period. Q1 · Final_v9_REALfinal2.xlsx

Malik coordinates next year's budget at Pelican Outfitters by emailing Budget.xlsx to six department heads. Two weeks later he has 14 reply-attachments; VP Susan edited an old copy, VP Marcus edited a different old copy, and their changes conflict in 30 cells. Malik spends a full Saturday merging by hand — and misses one of Susan's changes, which surfaces in the board meeting. What is the ROOT fix (not a bandage) for next year?

Answer: C. ✔ The disease is the traveling-copy model itself: every email send forks the truth, and no naming discipline can merge forks after the fact. Move to one cloud-hosted master and the forks become impossible — VPs edit THE budget, conflicts merge at the keystroke level, comments hold the arguments, and version history preserves every state Malik might need to prove later. ✘ A is a bandage on the fork model — v7-Susan and v7-Marcus still diverge, just with tidier names; naming conventions collapse the first busy week. ✘ B mistakes the symptom (file size) for the problem (copies); a 10 GB limit forks truth exactly as fast. ✘ D creates a human bottleneck: six VPs dictating edits to one coordinator is slower than the Saturday merge, and Malik becomes the single point of failure for the entire budget.

Q2 · Standup at 3 AM

Nova Analytics just hired engineers in Manila and Warsaw to join the Denver team. The daily 9:00 AM Denver video standup now lands at 11 PM in Manila and 5 PM in Warsaw; the Manila engineer has started sleeping through it, and honestly nobody blames her. The content is 90% "here's what I did / what I'll do / blockers." What should the team lead do?

Answer: A. ✔ Status reporting is the classic async workload: it needs a written record more than it needs eye contact, and async makes time zones irrelevant — everyone reads and writes in their own morning. Keeping a lighter twice-weekly sync preserves what synchronous time is actually FOR: negotiation, unblocking, riffing. This is the Segment 1 principle — choose the mode deliberately. ✘ B distributes the pain instead of removing it; rotating misery is still misery, now with scheduling chaos on top. ✘ C manages the symptom with authority: mandating 11 PM attendance buys resentment and sleepy compliance, not coordination. ✘ D throws out coordination itself; the failure wasn't having standups, it was running an async-shaped ritual in sync mode. Teams that cancel without a replacement rediscover why standups existed — via a missed dependency.

Q3 · The seventeen-email Thursday

Harbor Lane's events coordinator Maya must schedule author visits with publicists at five different publishing houses — outside companies whose calendars she cannot see. Last month's Tampa event took seventeen emails to find one mutually free hour. Her manager asks you, the consultant, for the cloud-native fix. Which do you recommend?

Answer: B. ✔ The booking page extends free/busy logic across organizational borders — the exact boundary where shared calendars stop working. Maya's availability is computed live from her calendar, publicists self-serve a slot, and the confirmation writes to both sides. Q5 · The $31,200 subscription

Using today's calculator, CFO Elena discovers the Monday "all-hands status sync" at her firm — 10 people × $60/hr loaded × 1 hour, every week — costs about $600 per occurrence, roughly $31,200 a year. Reviewing the recordings, she finds 50 of the 60 minutes are people reading updates aloud; decisions occupy the final 10. She doesn't want to lose the decisions. What's the calculator-informed redesign?

Answer: A. ✔ This is the sync/async split applied surgically: the read-aloud 50 minutes is async-shaped (status, needs a record, needs no back-and-forth), while the 10 decision minutes genuinely need synchronized humans. A 15-minute decision meeting costs ~$150/week ≈ $7,800/yr — about 75% saved, decisions intact, and the written updates become searchable history for free. ✘ B overcorrects: decisions are exactly the workload that earns synchronous time; canceling them trades $23k of savings for slow, ambiguous decision-making that costs more. ✘ C is the ostrich option — the cost continues whether or not the CFO looks at it. ✘ D is accounting comedy: total cost doubles to $1,200/occurrence while the per-person "metric" improves; if that logic worked, the cheapest meeting would be one the whole company attends.

Q8 · Death by five channels

In one enthusiastic quarter, Brightwave Media adopted Slack, a project board, a wiki, a new meetings tool, and kept email — five tools, each "the best in its category." Q9 · The decision nobody made 🧠 AI concept

Monday's AI-generated summary of Brightwave's product meeting reads: "DECISION: discontinue the Explorer product line (owner: Priya, effective Q3)." It's crisp, confident — and false. Priya wasn't even in the meeting, and the transcript shows only a muddled minute where two people talked over each other about "exploring line options." The summary is already filed in the project wiki. What property of the AI explains the error — and what's the right fix?

Answer: A. ✔ Confabulation, mechanically: the summarizer hit an ambiguous stretch and produced the most statistically likely shape for it — meeting summaries are FULL of "DECISION: … (owner: …)" lines, so that is what thin evidence became. The fix is workflow, not hope: a source-of-truth check against the recording, plus tiered sign-off before a summary becomes the filed record. ✘ B — the assistant queries no database of decisions; there was nothing to "pull," which is precisely the problem. ✘ C invents a villain when the known failure mode of generation explains everything — and the wiki timestamp matches the AI run, not a later edit. ✘ D overcorrects: the transcript wasn't wrong, just ambiguous; the answer is verification tiers, not banning a tool that saves hours.

Q10 · Four outputs, one reviewer 🧠 AI concept

Harbor Lane's ops team runs a stakes-based verification policy for AI output. Four items sit in this morning's queue: (1) an AI brainstorm of 30 author-event theme ideas the team will pick through; (2) an AI cleanup of Marcus's own meeting notes, for his own use; (3) an AI-drafted email to the landlord quoting "$18,400 in Q2 store-repair costs," assembled from photos of invoices; (4) a chat recap posted with the label "AI summary — correct me." It gets source verification and sign-off first, always. ✘ A — brainstorms are the canonical safe tier: bad ideas cost nothing because the humans picking through the list ARE the filter; reviewing 30 throwaway themes squanders the scarce reviewer. ✘ B — the only victim of an error in Marcus's own notes is Marcus, who rereads them; self-consumed output is the lowest tier. ✘ D — the "correct me" label is the trust matcher's as-is zone working as designed: it invites crowd-correction and creates no obligation, while item 3 puts real money on the line.

R2 · Five counties, one system — deployment models

Five neighboring Florida counties each need a records system that meets the same strict criminal-justice data rules (CJIS). None can afford to build a compliant private cloud alone; all five distrust generic public multi-tenant hosting for this data. Their IT directors meet at a conference and realize they have identical requirements. What deployment model was practically invented for this meeting?

Answer: C. ✔ Community cloud is defined by exactly this shape: multiple organizations, shared compliance regime (CJIS), shared costs, controls tailored to the group's common rules — private-cloud assurance at split-five pricing. ✘ A ignores the stated trust constraint; public cloud can host compliant workloads, but the scenario says these five require a narrower trust boundary than arbitrary co-tenants. ✘ B is the "none can afford it alone" option quintupled — five builds, five audit bills, zero sharing; it's the problem restated as a solution. ✘ D abuses vocabulary: hybrid means deliberately combining models per workload (private ledger + public burst); keeping everything on-prem and renaming it isn't hybrid, it's the status quo with a conference sticker.

R3 · The plant floor and the sales spike — hybrid

Ostara Manufacturing runs its factory-control systems on-premises — regulators and the plant engineers insist those never leave the building. Meanwhile its e-commerce analytics workload is bursty: quiet most of the month, then enormous for 72 hours after each product drop. The CIO wants one architecture strategy that respects both facts and connects the two worlds securely. What is she describing?

Answer: B. ✔ Hybrid's whole reason to exist: each workload placed where its requirements point — control and compliance keep the plant floor private; elasticity and pay-per-use fit the 72-hour bursts perfectly — with secure connectivity making them one system, which is what "one strategy" means. Most real enterprises land here (Session 1). ✘ A — community clouds serve shared requirements among allied orgs; Ostara's needs are internal, and competitors don't co-own plant-control infrastructure. ✘ C bets production machinery and the regulator relationship on "hope" — compliance isn't a mood. ✘ D solves the peak by buying hardware that idles ~27 days a month — the exact utilization disease (Session 1's 5–15% servers) that cloud economics exist to cure.

R4 · Nobody here patches anything — SaaS

Verdant Landscaping pays $23 per user per month for a CRM its crews open in a browser. The vendor hosts everything, applies every upgrade invisibly overnight, and Verdant's "IT department" is one part-timer who manages user accounts and imports data. During Week 1 of this course, Verdant's owner asks which service model he's been buying all along. It's:

Answer: D. ✔ Every clue is the SaaS checklist: browser access, per-user subscription, invisible provider-side upgrades, customer responsibility shrunk to data + users + settings. That's the top of the Session 1 stack diagram — YOU manage almost nothing. ✘ A — infrastructure absolutely exists underneath, but the service model is defined by the layer you buy at; Verdant never sees a server, an OS, or a patch. ✘ B — "platform" in PaaS means a place developers deploy their own code; storing your customer records in someone's finished app doesn't make it your platform. ✘ C confuses deployment with access control: tenant isolation and logins exist in every multi-tenant SaaS; a private cloud means infrastructure dedicated to one organization, which $23/user/month emphatically is not buying.

R5 · Push code, walk away — PaaS

Kite & Anchor's two developers deploy their web app by pushing code; the platform builds it, runs it, scales it when the app gets featured in a newsletter, and manages the database version underneath. They have never patched an operating system, never configured a load balancer, and couldn't tell you what OS their app runs on. In exchange, they must write the app the platform's way, and leaving the platform would mean rework. Which model — and what's the named trade-off?

Answer: C. ✔ Bring only code and data while the provider runs build, runtime, scaling, and managed services — that's Platform as a Service, and the sentence "leaving would mean rework" is vendor lock-in named precisely (Session 2's PaaS trade-off): each platform convenience is also a small hook. ✘ A inverts the facts — never patching an OS is the opposite of IaaS, where the OS upward is your job. ✘ B — SaaS is finished software you use; these developers are shipping software they wrote, one full layer down the stack. ✘ D is a deployment-model answer to a service-model question — and multi-tenancy (sharing the platform) is normal for public PaaS, not its defining trade-off.

R6 · Root access and 2 AM patches — IaaS

Meridian Games rents 14 virtual machines from a cloud provider by the hour. Their engineers chose the operating system images, they SSH in with root access, they schedule their own security patches (badly — last month's 2 AM patch window broke matchmaking), and they installed a custom game server the provider has never heard of. The provider supplies the hardware, virtualization layer, network, and storage. Which model is Meridian consuming?

Answer: A. ✔ Root access, OS choice, self-managed patching, arbitrary custom software: that's the IaaS deal — the provider's responsibility ends at the virtualization layer, and the broken 2 AM patch window is Meridian's very own (Session 3, plus the shared-responsibility preview for Session 8). ✘ B — if this were PaaS, Meridian would push game-server code and never see an OS; "we patch it ourselves" is disqualifying by definition. ✘ C plays word games: what Meridian sells players may be software-as-a-service, but what Meridian buys — the question asked — is raw infrastructure. Same company can sit on both sides. ✘ D — disaster recovery is a purpose, not this purchase; nothing in the scenario replicates systems for failover.

R7 · The board wants a number — cloud economics

Startup CFO Imani must present the financial case for launching on cloud infrastructure instead of buying $150,000 of servers her engineers sized "for where we might be in year three." The board's finance chair, a retired manufacturing executive, asks her to name the fundamental accounting shift, not just the totals. Her one-sentence answer should be:

Answer: D. ✔ The CapEx→OpEx shift, stated the way a finance chair hears it: no depreciating asset bought against a demand forecast, no year-three guess, spend correlated with reality, reversible monthly. For a startup whose real risk is being wrong about the future, that flexibility is the headline — more than any raw total. ✘ A overclaims and hands the chair an easy rebuttal: steady, fully-utilized workloads can favor owning (Session 1's Devon math showed rented totals exceeding purchase over 48 months). ✘ B states the shift backwards — a direction error that ends CFO credibility in one sentence. ✘ C — engineers still architect, secure, and monitor; cloud shifts their work from racking hardware to higher-value tasks, and "eliminates IT" is how boards end up surprised later.

R8 · Five apps, one gate — SSO

At Palmetto Credit, employee Jerome starts his day: signs in once at 8:30 (password + a push approval on his phone), then opens the loan system, email, the HR portal, the document vault, and the compliance tracker — no further login screens all morning. A new auditor asks the security team: "So where, exactly, does Jerome's password get checked, and how many of those five applications store it?" The correct answer:

Answer: B. ✔ This is SSO's architecture in one audit answer: authentication happens once, at the IdP (with MFA — the push), and applications outsource the question by trusting signed, expiring tokens. Password storage count across the five apps: zero — which shrinks the attack surface and gives security its one-click kill switch (Session 4's SSO animator, steps 3–7). ✘ A describes the pre-SSO disease: five password databases, five breach targets — exactly what the design eliminates. ✘ C would make app #1 an identity provider with a password-forwarding habit; forwarding credentials between applications is an anti-pattern federation exists to prevent. ✘ D — "the network remembers" isn't a control; sessions and tokens expire deliberately so that trust is always recent, revocable, and attributable.

R9 · Upgrade the second factor — MFA

After a phishing scare, Brightline Legal wants true two-factor login for its case-management system, upgraded specifically to resist phishing sites that relay codes in real time. The firm's four finalist schemes: (1) password + memorable passphrase; (2) password + emailed one-time link (email opens with the same password); (3) password + FIDO2 hardware security key; (4) fingerprint + face scan on the same phone. Which finalist delivers BOTH requirements?

Answer: C. ✔ Both boxes ticked: two different factor categories (know + have) make it true 2FA, and FIDO2's origin binding makes it phishing-resistant — the key signs a challenge tied to the real domain, so a pixel-perfect proxy site collects nothing replayable (Session 4's Renata scenario). ✘ A is one category twice — two "knows" fall to a single phishing page in a single paste. ✘ B is subtly awful: the "independent" email factor is guarded by the same password the phisher just captured, collapsing two factors into one; emailed links are also relayable in real time. ✘ D is two "ares" — one category — and adds biometrics' rotation problem: after compromise you can change a password, but not your face. Futuristic isn't a factor category.

R10 · The contractor who never left — provisioning

A security review at Cypress Health finds that Tariq, a billing contractor whose engagement ended eight months ago, still has a working VPN login and an active account in the claims system. Nobody used them — this time. The CISO wants the class of failure named and the class of fix proposed, in one line, for the board packet. Write the line.

Answer: A. ✔ Both halves are the classic pair (Session 4, Segment 3): the failure class is a leaver event that never propagated — the definition of an orphaned account, eight months of standing risk — and the fix class is structural: the HR/contract system emits the termination event, connectors revoke everywhere automatically, and periodic access reviews sweep for anything that slipped through. Contractors especially need end-dates, not end-memories. ✘ B secures the wrong thing: a strong password on an account that shouldn't exist is a well-locked door that shouldn't be there. ✘ C shoots the messenger — the review WORKED; the finding is the system functioning. ✘ D blames the tool: the VPN authenticated correctly per its records; no upgrade fixes "we never told it Tariq left."

R11 · Milliseconds matter — storage types

Suncrest Analytics is moving its transactional PostgreSQL database to the cloud. The engine rewrites small regions inside large open data files thousands of times a minute and needs consistent sub-millisecond disk latency. An intern proposes object storage ("cheapest per GB!"); a second intern proposes the company-wide shared file service ("everyone can see the data!"). The senior architect sighs and orders:

Answer: B. ✔ Databases are block storage's defining customer (Session 5): raw volumes deliver in-place partial writes at sub-millisecond latency, exactly matching an engine that surgically rewrites file interiors all day. Cloud block services (EBS-style) are literally "SAN as a service." ✘ A optimizes the wrong axis — object storage's eleven-nines durability is wonderful for the database's backups, but its whole-object-replacement-over-HTTP model can't do partial writes; the live database would crawl. ✘ C — a shared filesystem invites lock contention and latency, and "every department can see the raw DB files" is a governance incident, not a feature; departments get access through the database, not the filesystem. ✘ D confuses important with cold: archive means hours-long restores — a transactional database in the deep freezer isn't safe, it's off.

R12 · The auditor's three questions — tiers & 3-2-1

Meadowbrook Clinics keeps 4 TB of seven-year compliance records (accessed roughly twice a year) plus a live 600 GB patient-scheduling database. A new auditor asks three questions: Where do the old records live and at what cost profile? Is the database backup strategy resilient to ransomware? And can any administrator quietly alter the archived records? Which design answers all three well?

Answer: D. ✔ Question by question: twice-a-year records → archive tier (~$1/TB-month, hours-long restores are acceptable); ransomware resilience → 3-2-1 with versioned and immutable copies (an attack that encrypts and syncs can't reach a WORM copy, and versioning rewinds the rest); quiet alteration → object lock says no, even to admin credentials, until the retention clock expires. Quarterly test restores close the loop: "a backup isn't a backup until you've restored from it." ✘ A pays hot prices ~23× over, keeps the "backup" inside the blast radius, and leaves records admin-editable — failing all three questions. ✘ B is Session 1's flooded closet cosplaying as compliance. ✘ C repeats the exam's favorite trap: sync replicates the attack in seconds; without versioning there is no rewind — sync is not backup, in bold, forever.
