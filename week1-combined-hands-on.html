📝 Mixed Mastery Check

Six story problems spanning both sessions. Pick the best answer; every question explains all options after you answer.

Q1 · The flooded closet

Rosa's 14-person accounting firm keeps all client files on one server in a hallway closet, backed up "most Fridays" to a USB drive in the same room. A pipe bursts in July — both soaked, mid-tax-season. Which cloud characteristic would have MOST directly prevented the data loss?

Answer: C. ✔ Rosa's real failure is that every copy shared one room. Cloud storage keeps redundant copies across devices and regions in professionally managed data centers, so one burst pipe destroys nothing. ✘ A addresses capacity swings, not data survival. ✘ B is a billing property. ✘ D speeds provisioning after a disaster — too late; the files are already gone.

Q2 · Launch day at NoodleNow

Priya's food-delivery app normally serves 2,000 req/min on 4 servers. A viral video sends 40,000 req/min for six hours, then it's normal again. Her platform automatically grew to 80 servers and shrank back, billing only for hours used. Which characteristic did she experience?

Answer: D. ✔ Capacity stretched 20× automatically and contracted when demand fell (elasticity), billed by actual usage (measured service). ✘ A was present but didn't change capacity. ✘ B is the enabler underneath, not the behavior she experienced. ✘ C splits ONE big computation across machines — Priya had millions of small independent requests, the opposite shape.

Q3 · The apartment worry

A prospective SaaS customer objects: "Multi-tenant means my data is mixed in with my competitor's — no thanks." What is the accurate response?

Answer: B. ✔ Proper isolation is enforced on every query; a tenant can no more read a neighbor's rows than wander into the unit upstairs. The legitimate concerns are different: noisy neighbors (load) and the blast radius of a vendor-side bug. ✘ A confuses shared infrastructure with shared data. ✘ C misunderstands how SaaS works. ✘ D is invented.

Q4 · The git push team

Deshawn's five-person startup ships a custom ordering app weekly. They employ zero sysadmins and their differentiator is their code, not infrastructure. They want to git push and have the new version go live — built, deployed, and autoscaled — with no server to log into. Which model fits BEST?

Answer: A. ✔ "Bring only code, no server to log into, auto build/deploy/scale" is the exact PaaS promise. ✘ B gives finished software — but their product IS custom code, so SaaS can't host it. ✘ C/D both hand them the servers and OS to run — precisely the burden a five-person, zero-sysadmin team wants gone.

Q5 · The renewal shock

Two years in, a startup's PaaS vendor raises prices 40%. Migrating away means rewriting every feature built on the vendor's proprietary queue, database API, and auth SDK — months of work. What is this situation called, and when is it prevented?

Answer: B. ✔ Switching costs so high the vendor gains pricing power is vendor lock-in, and the mitigations are chosen while building, not while leaving. ✘ A/C/D name unrelated concepts. This is the same "how would we leave?" question that OpenSaaS answers on the SaaS side.

Q6 · The token-shaped bill 🧠 AI concept

Tanvi's startup added an AI chat helper via a big provider's model API. The first invoice is priced per million tokens. Her CFO asks: "Why can't we just buy the software once like our accounting package?" Which explanation is correct?

Answer: B. ✔ Every reply consumes real GPU compute at request time (inference), metered in tokens (~¾ of a word) — the cloud's measured-service trait applied to intelligence. ✘ A — a one-time license can't work: the seller pays compute every time Tanvi's customers chat. ✘ C — "token" is a text unit, not currency. ✘ D — answering never retrains the model; confusing training with inference is the classic mistake.
